1
DELAY
What they doThe longer a claim drags on, the more desperate you get. Desperate people settle for less. They "need more documentation," they're "waiting on a supervisor," the adjuster is "out of office." Every delay is strategic.
Your counterSet deadlines in writing. "If I do not receive a response within 7 business days, I will file a complaint with the Department of Insurance." Then do it. Deadlines without consequences are requests.
2
LOWBALL
FIRST OFFER
What they doFirst number is always 40-60% of what they've internally reserved. It's a test, not an offer. They expect you to negotiate. If you accept it, they win.
Your counterReject the first number entirely. Do not negotiate from it. Counter with your contractor's estimate. Make them argue against a professional assessment, not your opinion.
3
VAGUE
DENIALS
What they do"Does not meet criteria for coverage." They don't cite specific policy language because specific language can be challenged. Vagueness is a shield.
Your counterDemand the exact section, paragraph, and sentence they're relying on. "Please cite the specific policy language that supports your determination." 60% of the time, the language doesn't actually say what they claim.
4
DOCUMENT
FLOOD
What they doRequest unnecessary documentation. Receipts from 5 years ago. Proof you maintained the property. Anything that creates work. The hope: you give up before you finish.
Your counterAsk which specific policy provision requires each document. "Please identify the policy provision that makes this document necessary for claim evaluation." If they can't cite one, you don't need to provide it.
5
GOOD COP
/ BAD COP
What they doThe adjuster is "on your side" but their supervisor keeps denying things. The supervisor is "unavailable." They're not unavailable. They're the bad cop, and it's coordinated.
Your counterRequest the supervisor's direct contact information. In writing. "Please provide the name and direct phone number of the supervisor who made this determination." They'll either provide it or reverse the decision.
6
RECORDED
STATEMENT
What they doAsk for a recorded statement as if it's required. Every word will be parsed by people looking for a reason to deny or limit. "You said 'a few weeks' — that's maintenance, not sudden damage."
Your counterIt's optional. Decline. If you must give one, have your contractor's estimate in front of you. Don't guess. Don't speculate. Answer only what's asked. Stop talking.
7
DEADLINE
PRESSURE
What they do"This offer expires in 7 days." It never actually does. The deadline is artificial — a pressure tactic to make you accept before you've done your research.
Your counterIgnore the deadline. Get your own estimates. Take your time. A real settlement offer doesn't expire. If they truly pull it, you weren't going to get fair value anyway.
▸ THE BOTTOM LINE
Every one of these tactics works because policyholders don't know they're being played. Now you do. Document everything. Set deadlines. Escalate. Never take the first offer. Never give a recorded statement without preparation. And when in doubt, invoke the appraisal clause.
