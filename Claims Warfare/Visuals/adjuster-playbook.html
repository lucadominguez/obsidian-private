01
DELAY
The longer a claim drags on, the more desperate you get. Desperate people settle for less. They "need more documentation," the adjuster is "out of office," the supervisor is "reviewing." Every delay is calculated. Claims that close in 30 days average 22% higher payouts than claims that drag past 90 days.
Counter
Set deadlines in writing. "If I do not receive a response within 7 business days, I will file a complaint with the Department of Insurance." Then do it. Deadlines without consequences are requests.
02
LOWBALL FIRST OFFER
First number is always 40-60% of the internal reserve. It's a test, not an offer. The adjuster is measuring your reaction, your knowledge, your willingness to push back. If you accept or negotiate from their number, you've already lost the anchoring war.
Counter
Reject the first number entirely. Do not negotiate from it. Counter with your contractor's written estimate. Make them argue against a professional assessment, not your opinion. The first number on the table owns the negotiation.
03
VAGUE DENIALS
"Does not meet criteria for coverage." No specific policy language cited. No specific facts. Vagueness is a shield — specific language can be challenged, so they avoid it. 60% of the time, the policy language doesn't actually say what they're claiming.
Counter
Demand the exact section, paragraph, and sentence. "Please cite the specific policy language that supports your determination." This forces them to commit to a position in writing, and their position often falls apart under scrutiny.
04
DOCUMENT FLOOD
Request unnecessary documentation. Receipts from 5 years ago. Proof of maintenance records. Anything that creates work and delay. The strategy: make the process so burdensome you give up. Most people do.
Counter
Ask which specific policy provision requires each document. "Please identify the policy provision that makes this document necessary for claim evaluation." If they can't cite one, you don't need to provide it.
05
GOOD COP / BAD COP
The adjuster is "on your side" but their supervisor keeps denying things. The supervisor is conveniently "unavailable" when you want to escalate. This is coordinated. The adjuster and supervisor discuss every claim. The "bad cop" is deliberate.
Counter
Request the supervisor's direct contact information in writing. "Please provide the name and direct phone number of the supervisor who made this determination." They'll either provide it and you escalate, or they'll reverse the decision to avoid escalation.
06
RECORDED STATEMENT TRAP
They ask for a recorded statement as if it's required. The form uses official language. It's optional in most cases. Every word will be parsed by people whose job is finding a reason to pay less. "You said 'a few weeks' — that's maintenance, not sudden damage."
Counter
Decline. If you must give one, have your contractor's estimate in front of you. Don't guess. Don't speculate. Answer only what's asked. Stop talking. A written statement you've reviewed is always safer than a recording.
07
DEADLINE PRESSURE
"This offer expires in 7 days." It never actually does. The deadline is entirely artificial — a pressure tactic designed to make you accept before you've done your research, gotten your own estimates, or consulted anyone. Urgency is the enemy of good decisions.
Counter
Ignore the deadline. Get your own estimates. Take your time. A real settlement offer doesn't expire. If they truly pull it, you weren't going to get fair value anyway. The only real deadlines are the ones in your policy or state law.

▸ THE BOTTOM LINE
Every one of these tactics works because policyholders don't know they're being played. Document everything. Set deadlines. Escalate. Never take the first offer. Never give a recorded statement without preparation. And when in doubt, invoke the appraisal clause. The system is designed against you. Now you know how it works.
