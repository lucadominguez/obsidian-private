# Free vs Paid Strategy

## The Core Principle

> Free content proves you know what you're talking about. Paid content is the playbook they can't get anywhere else.

The free reader should finish every article thinking: *"This person actually knows how this works. If the free stuff is this good, the paid stuff must be incredible."*

## What Goes Where

### Always Free
Content that:
- Establishes credibility and insider status
- Is shareable (emotional hooks, counterintuitive insights)
- Drives email capture
- Has broad appeal across claim types

**Examples:**
- "I Was an Insurance Adjuster for 12 Years. Here's What I Wish You Knew."
- "The 5 Words That Will Get Your Claim Denied"
- "How Adjusters Are Trained to Evaluate Your Damage"
- "What Your Insurance Company's Lawyers Don't Want You to Know"
- Claim horror stories with specific lessons extracted

### Always Paid
Content that:
- Is the actual step-by-step execution playbook
- Contains scripts, templates, and exact language
- Is claim-type specific (roof, water, fire, auto)
- Would cost thousands if you hired a public adjuster or attorney
- Requires ongoing access (regulations change, new tactics emerge)

**Examples:**
- "The Complete Roof Claim Playbook: From Inspection to Settlement"
- "Water Damage Documentation: The Room-by-Room Protocol"
- "Adjuster Negotiation Scripts: 47 Exact Phrases That Work"
- "Business Interruption Claims: Calculating What They Actually Owe You"
- "The Denial Appeal System: Templates, Timelines, and Escalation Paths"

### The Bridge Content
Articles that start free and naturally lead to paid:
1. Free article explains the concept and gives 40% of the value
2. Natural conclusion: "To actually execute this, you need the full playbook"
3. Paid version includes: templates, scripts, decision trees, state-specific guidance

**Example Bridge:**
> Free: "How Adjusters Calculate Roof Damage Payouts" (explains the formula, the games they play, what to watch for)
> 
> Paid: "Roof Claim Playbook" (your exact script for the adjuster walkthrough, damage documentation templates, the 3-tier negotiation strategy, state-specific depreciation rules, the appraisal clause trigger letter)

## Content Ratio

| Category | % of Output | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Pure free | 40% | Authority building, sharing, email capture |
| Bridge content | 35% | Demonstrate depth, create paywall urgency |
| Pure paid | 25% | Core value, retention, justification of price |

## The Paywall Positioning

Not: "Pay to read more."
But: "The free article gave you the map. The playbook gives you the keys, the car, and someone who's driven this route a hundred times."

The subscriber is not paying for *information*. They're paying for:
- **Confidence** — knowing they're doing it right when $50,000 is on the line
- **Specificity** — not general advice, but "here's exactly what to say to your adjuster on Tuesday"
- **Ongoing access** — claims take months. They need you for the duration.
- **The inside game** — you know things that cannot be Googled

## Pricing Psychology

$149-599/mo works because:
- A public adjuster takes 10-20% of the claim (on a $100,000 claim, that's $10,000-$20,000)
- An attorney takes 33-40%
- Your subscriber pays $149-599/mo for 3-12 months = $447-$7,188 total
- They save $10,000+ and keep control of their claim
- The math is obvious

The subscription isn't an expense. It's the cheapest insurance they'll ever buy.
