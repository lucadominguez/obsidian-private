# What Your Adjuster Knows That You Don't

**Status:** Published (Free)
**Series:** Foundation
**Lead Magnet:** "Damage Documentation Checklist"

---

For twelve years, I was the person you dreaded seeing pull into your driveway.

Clipboard. Measuring tape. A handheld device that calculated depreciation faster than your contractor could explain why the repair would cost more. I was polite about it. Professional, even. And I was trained — systematically, methodically, over eight weeks of classroom instruction and months of field shadowing — to pay you less than your claim was worth.

I wasn't evil. Most of the time I wasn't even dishonest. I was doing my job. The problem was the job itself: a set of incentives so perfectly, quietly misaligned with your interests that you never saw them working. Every dollar I didn't authorize was a dollar my employer kept. Every claim I closed under reserve was a metric that came up at my quarterly review. Nobody ever told me to lowball anyone. They didn't have to. The training, the software, the scripts, the performance metrics — all of it pushed in the same direction, and the direction was down.

I left in 2022 after watching a single mother get her roof claim denied on a technicality. The damage was real. The denial was bullshit. She just didn't know the magic words.[^1] I wrote the denial letter myself. I can still see her name on the header.

[^1]: I am being literal here. There are specific phrases — "bad faith claims handling," "I am requesting a re-inspection by a different adjuster," "please cite the exact policy language you are relying on" — that trigger different internal workflows than ordinary complaints. The magic words are real. We'll get to them.

Here's what I learned in those twelve years, handling something like 8,000 claims across property, auto, and commercial lines: the system isn't broken. It's working exactly as designed. It's just designed against you.

---

## The training

Eight weeks. That's how long they spent teaching me to evaluate claims before they let me near a real one.

Week one was policy language — exclusions, limitations, conditions. We learned to read a policy the way a prosecutor reads a statute: looking for what's *not* covered, what's *capped*, what's *conditional*. By week two, I could glance at a new claim and mentally flag the three or four clauses that would limit the payout before I'd seen the damage.

Week three was documentation. Photos. Measurements. Statements. Specifically: how to document the evidence that supports the company's position. The angle of the photo matters. The framing of the question matters. "When did you first notice the leak?" is a different question from "When did the leak start?" One of them implies you should have noticed earlier. The other doesn't. I was taught the difference in week three and I used it for twelve years.

Week four was negotiation. The good-cop routine. The strategic pause after making an offer — let the silence do the work, let them negotiate against themselves. The way to phrase a lowball so it sounds like a concession: "I was able to get this approved" instead of "this is what we're offering." Same number. Completely different frame.

Not one hour of that eight weeks covered how to maximize a policyholder's payout. Not one.

## How the game actually works

Your adjuster is not evaluating your claim against what it will cost to fix the damage. They are evaluating your claim against what they think they can get you to accept. The gap between these two numbers has an industry name — "reserve leakage" — and minimizing it is, quietly, the actual job.

This took me a few years to fully understand, because nobody says it out loud, but once you see it you can't unsee it. The adjuster's performance is measured on three metrics:

1. **Cycle time** — how fast claims get closed
2. **Severity** — the average payout per claim type (lower is better)
3. **File quality** — documentation that would survive an audit

Policyholder satisfaction is not on the list. Accurate valuation of damage is not on the list. Fairness is not on the list.

The first offer they make you is a test.[^2] It's not the real number. It's a probe — the adjuster is measuring your reaction, your knowledge level, your willingness to push back. If you accept the first offer, or negotiate *from* it, you've already lost. The real negotiation starts when you reject the number entirely and counter with documentation they can't dismiss.

[^2]: I know this because I was trained to make that offer. The first number was usually 40-60% of what we'd internally reserved for the claim. The policy wasn't to lowball — the policy was to leave room to "negotiate" while anchoring the conversation to a number far below actual damages. If they accepted the first offer, great. If they negotiated up to 70%, we still came out ahead of what we'd budgeted.

I had quarterly bonuses tied to my severity numbers. So did every adjuster I worked with. Nobody ever mentioned this to a claimant.

## Four things they trained me never to volunteer

**1. You can request a different adjuster.**

If the person assigned to your claim is dismissive, unresponsive, or clearly lowballing, call the claims department and ask for a reassignment. You don't need a legal standard. "I don't feel this adjuster is evaluating my claim fairly" is sufficient — and it will trigger a different internal process than ordinary complaints, because the company now has a written record that you've alleged unfair treatment. Most policyholders don't know this exists. The company will never tell you.

**2. The appraisal clause is a nuclear option.**

It's buried in most homeowner policies. If you and the insurer disagree on the loss amount, either party can invoke appraisal: you hire an appraiser, they hire an appraiser, the two appraisers pick an umpire, and the decision of any two of the three is binding. This bypasses your adjuster entirely. I watched claims that had been stalled for six months resolve in three weeks once appraisal was invoked — because suddenly my opinion wasn't the final word anymore.[^3]

[^3]: Appraisal isn't free — each side pays their own appraiser and splits the umpire's cost. For a $50,000 disputed claim, your share might run $2,000-4,000. But compare that to accepting their "final" offer of $25,000 when the actual damages are $50,000. The math isn't close. The adjuster knows this and counts on you not knowing.

**3. "This is the most I can authorize" is never true.**

Every adjuster has a personal settlement authority limit — usually $10,000 to $25,000 for residential claims. But their supervisor has more. Their supervisor's manager has more still. A VP can authorize essentially anything. "This is my final offer" means "this is the highest I personally can go without getting someone else involved." Escalate past the person making the offer and the ceiling disappears.

**4. The recorded statement is a trap.**

Adjusters ask for one as if it's required. The form they send you will say "request for recorded statement" in official-looking type. It's optional in most cases. You are not obligated to give one. And you probably shouldn't.[^4]

[^4]: Unless your policy explicitly requires cooperation in the investigation — read your policy. Some do. Most don't require a *recorded* statement specifically. But even when cooperation is required, you can cooperate in writing. A written statement you've reviewed and edited is always safer than a recording where everything you say, including the natural verbal imprecision that happens in conversation, becomes evidence.

The recorded statement is where adjusters harvest the ammunition they'll use to limit or deny your claim. You say "the leak probably started a few weeks ago" — the policy requires "sudden and accidental" discharge. If it was leaking for weeks, that's maintenance, not a covered loss. You say "I think the damage is around $15,000" — congratulations, you just anchored your own negotiation to a number you pulled out of the air. Every word you speak in a recorded statement will be parsed by people whose entire job is finding a reason to pay you less.

If you must give one, have your contractor's estimate in front of you. Don't guess. Don't speculate. Answer only what's asked. Stop talking.

## The one thing that changes everything

If I had to reduce twelve years of claims handling to one rule, it would be: **document everything as if you're building a court case, not filing an insurance claim.**

The policyholder who internalizes this and the policyholder who doesn't live in different worlds. The first one gets paid at or near full value. The second one takes whatever's offered because they don't have the evidence to push back and they don't know they're allowed to.

Here's what it looks like:

- Photos of the damage, obviously. But also photos of the *undamaged* areas — so the adjuster can't claim the damage was pre-existing by pointing to "similar conditions elsewhere on the property." I used that argument routinely. It worked because most people only photograph what's broken.
- A written log of every interaction: date, time, name, what they said, what you said. Adjusters keep detailed notes. Their notes will be used to justify their decisions. Your notes should be equally detailed and you should tell them you're keeping them.
- Contractor estimates *before* the adjuster's estimate. The first number on the table becomes the anchor. If that number is theirs, you're negotiating in their frame. Get three quotes from licensed contractors and hand them the highest one. Make them argue against a professional estimate instead of your guess.
- Photos with timestamps. With measurements. From multiple angles. I cannot tell you how many claims I limited because the policyholder sent three blurry photos of a roof and expected me to authorize a full replacement. I needed evidence to justify the payout to *my* supervisor. If you don't give me that evidence, my hands are tied — or at least that's what I'll tell you.

Most people approach a claim like customer service. They expect the insurance company to do the right thing, evaluate the damage fairly, and pay what's owed. That expectation is reasonable. It's also a strategic error.

A claim is an adversarial negotiation between parties with opposing financial interests. The sooner you treat it like one, the better your outcome will be.
