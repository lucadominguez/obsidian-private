# What Your Adjuster Knows That You Don't

**Status:** Published (Free)  
**Series:** Foundation  
**Word count:** ~1,800  
**Read time:** 7 minutes  

---

For twelve years, I was the person you dreaded seeing at your front door.

I carried a clipboard and a measuring tape and a handheld device that calculated depreciation faster than your contractor could explain why the repair would cost more. I was polite. I was professional. And I was trained to save my company money on your claim.

I wasn't evil. I wasn't even dishonest, most of the time. I was doing my job — a job whose incentives were perfectly, quietly misaligned with yours. Every dollar I didn't authorize was a dollar my employer kept. Every claim I closed under reserve was a metric that made me look good at my quarterly review. Nobody ever told me to lowball anyone. They didn't have to. The systems, the training, the software, the scripts — all of it pushed in the same direction.

I left that job three years ago. And I've spent those three years watching policyholders make the same mistakes I used to count on.

---

## The training they don't tell you about

When I was hired as an adjuster, I went through an eight-week training program. Not one hour of it covered how to maximize a policyholder's payout. Not one.

Week one was policy language. Specifically: exclusions, limitations, and conditions. We learned to read a policy the way a prosecutor reads a statute — looking for what's *not* covered, what's *capped*, what's *conditional*. By week two, we could glance at a claim and mentally check off the three or four clauses that would limit the payout before we'd even seen the damage.

Week three was documentation. Photos. Measurements. Statements. Not how to document the full extent of the damage — how to document the evidence that supports the company's position. The angle of the photo matters. The framing of the question matters. "When did you first notice the leak?" is a different question than "When did the leak start?" One of them implies you should have noticed earlier. The other doesn't. We were taught the difference.

Week four was negotiation. The "good cop" routine. The "this is the best I can do" script. The strategic pause after making an offer — let the silence do the work. The way to phrase a lowball so it sounds like a favor. "I was able to get this approved" instead of "this is what we're offering."

None of this was secret. It was just never shared with you.

## How the game actually works

Here's something most people don't understand: your adjuster is not evaluating your claim against what it will cost to fix the damage. They are evaluating your claim against what they think they can get you to accept.

The difference between those two numbers is called "reserve leakage." And minimizing it is, quietly, the actual job.

The first offer is never the real number. It's a test. The adjuster is measuring your reaction, your knowledge, your willingness to push back. If you accept the first offer — or negotiate from it — you've already lost. The real negotiation starts when you reject the first number entirely and counter with documentation they can't dismiss.

Adjusters are evaluated on three metrics:
1. **Cycle time** — how fast they close claims
2. **Severity** — the average payout per claim type
3. **File quality** — documentation that would hold up if the claim were audited

Notice what's missing from that list. "Policyholder satisfaction." "Accurate valuation of damage." "Fairness."

The incentive structure rewards adjusters who close claims quickly at the lowest number the policyholder will accept. Not the number the damage actually costs to repair. The number you'll take.

## What they hope you don't know

Here are four things I was trained to never volunteer:

**1. You can request a different adjuster.**

If the adjuster assigned to your claim is dismissive, unresponsive, or clearly lowballing, you can call the claims department and ask for a reassignment. You don't need a reason that meets some legal standard. "I don't feel this adjuster is evaluating my claim fairly" is enough. Most people don't know this. The company will not tell you.

**2. The appraisal clause exists.**

Buried in most homeowner policies is something called the appraisal clause. If you and the insurance company disagree on the value of the damage, either party can invoke appraisal. Each side hires an independent appraiser. The two appraisers select an umpire. The decision of any two of the three is binding. This bypasses the adjuster entirely. I watched claims that had been stalled for months resolve in weeks once appraisal was invoked — because suddenly the adjuster's opinion wasn't the final word anymore.

**3. Your adjuster has settlement authority limits — but their supervisor has more.**

The adjuster will tell you "this is the maximum I can authorize." What they mean is "this is the maximum *I personally* can authorize." Their supervisor has a higher limit. Their supervisor's manager has an even higher one. Every "final offer" is final only until you escalate past the person making it.

**4. The recorded statement is optional.**

Adjusters will ask for a recorded statement as if it's required. It's not. In most cases, you are not obligated to give one. And you probably shouldn't — not without understanding exactly what they're looking for. The recorded statement is where adjusters collect the ammunition they'll use to limit or deny your claim. "You said the leak started 'a few weeks ago' — but the policy requires 'sudden and accidental' discharge. If it was leaking for weeks, that's a maintenance issue." Every word you say in a recorded statement will be parsed by people whose job is to find a reason to pay you less.

## The one thing that makes the biggest difference

If I had to compress twelve years of insider knowledge into a single sentence, it would be this:

**Document everything as if you're building a court case, not filing an insurance claim.**

The policyholder who takes this seriously and the policyholder who doesn't live in different worlds. The first one gets their claim paid at or near full value. The second one takes whatever is offered because they don't have the evidence to push back.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

- Photos of the damage, yes. But also photos of the *undamaged* areas — so the adjuster can't claim the damage was pre-existing by pointing to "similar conditions elsewhere."
- A written log of every call: date, time, who you spoke to, what they said, what you said. Adjusters keep notes. So should you.
- Contractor estimates *before* the adjuster's estimate — so you're negotiating from your number, not theirs.
- Photos with timestamps. Photos with measurements. Photos from multiple angles. I cannot tell you how many claims I limited because the policyholder had three blurry photos of a roof and expected me to authorize a full replacement.

Most people approach a claim like a customer service issue. They expect the insurance company to do the right thing, document the damage fairly, and pay what's owed. That expectation is understandable. It's also a mistake.

A claim is an adversarial negotiation. The sooner you treat it like one, the better your outcome will be.
