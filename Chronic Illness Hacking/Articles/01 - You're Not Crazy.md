# You're Not Crazy. You're Not Alone. And There Are Things That Help.

**Published:** Week 1, Monday — Flagship article
**Type:** Free — Authority-building, emotional core
**Lead Magnet:** "The 12 Lab Tests Your Doctor Hasn't Ordered"

---

I spent three years in bed.

Not resting. Not "taking it easy." In bed. Horizontal. The kind of horizontal where you measure your day in trips to the bathroom and whether you managed to eat something before the exhaustion swallowed you again.

The doctors called it depression. They prescribed antidepressants that made it worse — the kind of worse where you can't even cry anymore, you just lie there feeling chemically flattened. They told me to exercise, which triggered crashes that lasted weeks. They told me my labs were normal. They told me, in so many words, that it was in my head.

I wasn't depressed. I mean, I was — you try not being depressed after three years of your life disappearing — but the depression was downstream of something else. Something mainstream medicine didn't have a name for, or didn't believe in, or both. I had myalgic encephalomyelitis. ME/CFS. A neuroimmune condition that affects somewhere between 17 and 24 million people worldwide and that most doctors receive precisely zero hours of training on.[^1]

[^1]: This is not an exaggeration. A 2018 survey of US medical schools found that fewer than 30% included *any* content on ME/CFS in their curriculum, and those that did averaged less than an hour. The condition is more common than multiple sclerosis, HIV, and many cancers, and receives a fraction of the research funding. The NIH allocates roughly $15 million annually for ME/CFS research. For comparison, multiple sclerosis — which affects about a third as many people — receives over $100 million.

I researched my way back. Not because I'm special — because I was desperate, and desperation is a surprisingly effective research methodology when the alternative is your life slowly evaporating. I found doctors who actually listened. I tried protocols based on published research and patient experience reports: mitochondrial support, nervous system retraining, off-label medications, targeted supplementation. Some worked. Some didn't. I documented everything in a spreadsheet that eventually became the foundation of this community.

Today I'm at about 80% function. I work part-time. I exercise carefully — never above 60% of my max heart rate, because exceeding that threshold still triggers crashes. I have a life again. Not the life I had before — I'm not going to pretend this illness doesn't leave permanent marks — but a life worth living.

This article is not medical advice. I'm not a doctor. I'm a patient who spent three years bedridden and four years methodically climbing out, and what follows is what I learned along the way.

## The gaslighting is part of the illness

Before I get to the protocols, I need to talk about the thing that almost broke me before the illness did.

Every doctor I saw — and I saw twelve of them over three years — ran the same standard panel. CBC, CMP, TSH, maybe an ANA if they were feeling thorough. The results came back normal. And every single one of them drew the same conclusion: if the labs are normal, the patient must be fine. The symptoms must be psychological. "Have you considered that this might be stress-related?"

Here's the problem with that logic. The standard lab panel was designed to catch acute illness and obvious pathology. It was never designed to detect the low-grade immune dysregulation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and autonomic nervous system collapse that characterize conditions like ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, POTS, MCAS, and long COVID. Running a standard panel on someone with a complex chronic illness is like trying to diagnose a computer virus by checking if the power cord is plugged in. The test isn't wrong. It's just testing the wrong thing.

When I finally found a doctor who ordered the *right* tests, the picture changed instantly. My EBV panel showed reactivation. My natural killer cell function was at the 5th percentile.[^2] My tilt table test was positive for POTS — my heart rate jumped 40 beats per minute just from standing up. My tryptase was elevated, pointing toward mast cell activation. None of this showed up on the standard panels I'd been getting for three years.

[^2]: Low natural killer cell function is one of the most consistent findings in ME/CFS research. It's not diagnostic on its own — plenty of healthy people have low NK function — but in combination with the symptom pattern and other markers, it's a strong signal. The test exists. Almost no one orders it.

I wasn't crazy. I had a multi-system illness that the standard diagnostic toolkit was never designed to find. The gaslighting — and I'm using that word deliberately, because being told repeatedly that your lived experience of your own body is wrong is a form of gaslighting — was not a failure of individual doctors. It was a failure of the diagnostic paradigm.

## The tests that actually matter

If you're dealing with an invisible chronic illness and you've been told your labs are normal, here are the tests that changed everything for me and for the members of this community. These are not standard. You will have to ask for them, and some doctors will refuse. Find one who won't.

**Early morning cortisol.** Not the random cortisol you get in a standard panel, which is useless because cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm. You need an 8 AM draw, and ideally a four-point salivary cortisol test that tracks the full daily curve. Many people with ME/CFS and related conditions show a flattened cortisol rhythm — not high enough in the morning, not low enough at night.

**Full thyroid panel.** TSH alone is a screening test, not a diagnostic. You need free T3, free T4, reverse T3, and thyroid antibodies. I've lost count of how many members have "normal" TSH and free T3 at the absolute bottom of the range, with reverse T3 elevated. That's functional hypothyroidism. It won't show up on a standard panel.

**Ferritin.** Not just iron. Ferritin. The standard range starts at 15 ng/mL. Optimal is 70-100. I was at 22 and told I was fine. I was not fine. Low ferritin is shockingly common in chronic illness, especially in women, and it produces symptoms — fatigue, brain fog, restless legs, exercise intolerance — that overlap almost perfectly with the conditions themselves.

**EBV panel.** Epstein-Barr virus reactivation is common in ME/CFS and long COVID. The test exists. Most doctors won't run it unless you specifically ask. You need the full panel: VCA IgG, VCA IgM, and EBNA (Epstein-Barr nuclear antigen). The pattern of these three tells you whether the infection is past, current, or reactivated.

**Vitamin D (25-hydroxy).** Not the active form. The storage form. I was at 19 ng/mL on a reference range that starts at 30. "Mildly low" was the note. Vitamin D is a steroid hormone precursor that modulates immune function and hundreds of genes. "Mildly low" is not acceptable when your immune system is already dysregulated.

**ANA + specific autoantibodies.** A positive ANA alone is nonspecific. But if you have a positive ANA and symptoms consistent with autoimmunity, you need the follow-up panel: anti-dsDNA, anti-Ro/SSA, anti-La/SSB, anti-RNP, anti-Smith, anti-cardiolipin. Autoimmune conditions are dramatically underdiagnosed in patients labeled with "chronic fatigue" or "fibromyalgia."

**Tryptase.** Mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS) is increasingly recognized as a comorbidity in ME/CFS, POTS, and EDS. Elevated tryptase is a marker. The test is a blood draw. It's straightforward. Almost no one runs it.

**Tilt table test or NASA lean test.** POTS — postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome — is present in a significant percentage of ME/CFS patients. The diagnostic criterion is a heart rate increase of 30+ bpm within 10 minutes of standing. You can do a poor man's version at home with a pulse oximeter. If your heart rate jumps and stays elevated, you need a formal evaluation.

I spent approximately $4,000 on specialist visits over three years, and not one of them ordered most of these tests. When I finally went to a discount lab and paid $300 out of pocket for the full panel, the results explained my entire clinical picture in about 20 minutes of reading.

## What actually moved the needle

I'm not going to tell you I found a miracle cure. I didn't. What I found was a series of partial improvements that compounded over time, each one unlocking a little more function, until the cumulative effect was a life I recognized again.

**Low-dose naltrexone (LDN).** This was the single biggest intervention for me. LDN is exactly what it sounds like — naltrexone, the opioid antagonist used for addiction treatment, at roughly 1/10th the standard dose. At low doses, it appears to work through a completely different mechanism: transiently blocking opioid receptors, which triggers a rebound increase in endorphin production, which modulates the immune system and reduces neuroinflammation.[^3] I started at 0.5mg and worked up to 4.5mg over six weeks. The effect was not dramatic — it was gradual, a slow lifting of the worst of the fatigue and brain fog. But it was real.

[^3]: The mechanism is still being worked out, but the leading hypothesis involves LDN's effect on toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on microglia — the immune cells of the central nervous system. By blocking TLR4 signaling, LDN appears to reduce the neuroinflammatory component of ME/CFS. There are now several small trials showing benefit, and a larger one is underway at Stanford. LDN is not FDA-approved for ME/CFS and must be prescribed off-label or obtained through compounding pharmacies.

**Pacing.** This is simultaneously the simplest and hardest intervention. Pacing means staying within your energy envelope — never exceeding the threshold that triggers post-exertional malaise, the crash that follows overexertion and can last days or weeks. I use a heart rate monitor to stay below 60% of my max heart rate. This means I stop doing things before I feel tired. It means saying no constantly. It means accepting that your capacity is real and finite and that exceeding it has consequences. Pacing is not a treatment. It's a management strategy. But it's the foundation that makes every other intervention possible.

**Mitochondrial support.** The evidence for mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS is growing, and the interventions are relatively low-risk: CoQ10 (ubiquinol form, 200mg daily), NADH (10mg sublingual), acetyl-L-carnitine (500mg twice daily), and D-ribose (5g, three times daily during crashes). I can't tell you which of these helped most because I started them all at roughly the same time — a methodological sin, but I was desperate and not in a clinical trial. The combination seemed to improve my energy baseline by maybe 15-20%. Not life-changing on its own. But in a condition where every percentage point matters, 15-20% is meaningful.

**Nervous system retraining.** This is controversial. Programs like DNRS, Gupta, and ANS Rewire make claims that some people find excessive. I approached them with deep skepticism. What I found was that the approach — which essentially involves redirecting the brain's threat response away from the body's sensations — did seem to reduce my hypersensitivity to symptoms and, over time, my actual symptom burden. I don't think it "rewired" my nervous system in any literal sense. I think it reduced the secondary amplification that chronic illness creates, where the fear of symptoms becomes a symptom itself. Whatever the mechanism, it helped.

**Finding the right doctor.** This is not a protocol. It's a prerequisite. I found a functional medicine practitioner who had actually treated ME/CFS patients before. She ordered the tests my previous doctors hadn't. She was willing to prescribe off-label. She didn't tell me it was in my head. The transformation in my care came not from any single intervention but from finally having a clinician who treated my condition as real and complex and worth investigating.

## What I know now that I wish I'd known then

Your labs are probably not normal. They're normal against reference ranges that include sick people and weren't designed to detect the conditions you're dealing with. Get the right tests.

Recovery is not a straight line. It's a jagged mess of improvements and setbacks, and the setbacks feel like failure but they're not. They're data. Every crash tells you something about your limits. Every protocol that doesn't work eliminates a variable. The spreadsheet I kept for four years is the most valuable thing I own, not because it shows a clean recovery arc but because it shows the real one.

You're not alone. I know how isolating this illness is — the friends who stop calling, the family members who don't believe you, the doctors who make you feel like you're wasting their time. But there are millions of us, and we're organizing, and we're building the datasets that the medical system should have built decades ago.

This community exists because the information that helped me was scattered across forums, research papers, and the brains of a handful of specialists. I'm organizing it all in one place. The protocol database, the doctor recommendation list, the supplement experience reports — every piece of it comes from people who have actually lived through these conditions and documented what happened.

You're not crazy. You're not alone. And there are things that help. You just haven't found them yet.
