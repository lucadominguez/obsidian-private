## Summary

Standard diagnostic panels are designed for acute illness, not the complex immune, autonomic, and metabolic dysfunction characteristic of ME/CFS, fibromyalgia, POTS, MCAS, and long COVID. This briefing identifies the tests that detect these conditions — tests that are available, validated, and almost never ordered.

---

## Key Finding: The Diagnostic Gap

Baraniuk et al. (2024, *Frontiers in Immunology*, DOI: 10.3389/fimmu.2024.1440643) conducted a meta-analysis of 28 studies and found natural killer cell cytotoxicity reduced to roughly half of healthy control levels in ME/CFS — the most consistent immune abnormality in the literature. The test exists. It is not in any standard panel. This gap between what medicine can measure and what it routinely does measure is the central problem this publication exists to address.

## Tests Standard Panels Miss

| Test | What It Detects | Why It's Missed |
|---|---|---|
| Early morning cortisol (4-point salivary) | Flattened diurnal rhythm common in ME/CFS | Standard panels use random cortisol |
| Full thyroid panel (TSH + fT3 + fT4 + rT3 + antibodies) | Functional hypothyroidism with normal TSH | Standard panels use TSH alone |
| Ferritin | Iron deficiency without anemia | Standard panels use hemoglobin |
| EBV panel (VCA IgG/IgM + EBNA) | Viral reactivation | Not on standard panels |
| Tryptase | Mast cell activation (MCAS) | Not on standard panels |
| Tilt table / NASA lean test | POTS (30+ bpm increase on standing) | Not on standard panels |
| NK cell function | Immune dysfunction | Not on standard panels |
| ANA + specific autoantibodies | Autoimmune conditions | ANA alone is nonspecific |

A 2018 survey found fewer than 30% of US medical schools include any content on ME/CFS, averaging less than one hour. The NIH allocates approximately $15M annually for ME/CFS research — a condition affecting 17-24 million people. Multiple sclerosis, affecting about a third as many, receives over $100M.

## Interventions with Database Support

Low-dose naltrexone (LDN, 0.5-4.5mg): The most frequently reported intervention in the patient database. Toljan & Vrooman (2018, *Medical Sciences*, DOI: 10.3390/medsci6040082) describe the dual TLR4 antagonism + opioid receptor modulation mechanism. Fibromyalgia: -28.8% pain reduction (Patten et al., 2018). Multiple sclerosis: +9 SF-36 points.

Pacing with heart rate monitoring: staying below 60% of max heart rate prevents post-exertional malaise. The database shows this is the highest-compliance, lowest-risk intervention — and the most psychologically difficult to maintain.

## Implications

1. The diagnostic gap is structural, not scientific. The tests exist. They are not ordered because medical training doesn't cover the conditions they detect.
2. Patient-organized data is often more clinically useful than published literature for conditions pharma doesn't study.
3. The gaslighting — being told repeatedly that normal labs mean you're fine — is a failure of the diagnostic paradigm, not of individual doctors.
