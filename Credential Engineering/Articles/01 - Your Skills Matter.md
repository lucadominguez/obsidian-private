**Classification:** Free

## Summary

Approximately 2 million college-educated immigrants in the US are unemployed or underemployed — a phenomenon the Migration Policy Institute calls "brain waste." The single largest driver is non-recognition of foreign credentials. This briefing documents the pathways that bypass this barrier.

---

## The Scale

The education industry generates $1.7 trillion annually. The average bachelor's degree costs $104,000 (private) or $41,000 (public). Meanwhile, credential evaluation through a NACES member evaluator costs approximately $200 and takes 7-10 business days. A credential evaluation report legally establishes your foreign degree as equivalent to a US bachelor's for employment and licensing purposes.

## The Negotiation Script

When an interviewer questions foreign credentials, the database shows the most effective response pattern:

> "My degree is from [University] in [Country], which has been evaluated as equivalent to a US bachelor's in [Field] by World Education Services. I have the evaluation report if you'd like to see it. More importantly, here's what I've built."

Three elements: (1) name a specific, recognized evaluation service, (2) offer the report — signaling confidence, not defensiveness, (3) immediately pivot to demonstrated skills and output. The degree is a checkbox. The work is what they're hiring.

---

## Analysis

The credential barrier is a paperwork problem, not a skills problem. The information needed to bypass it exists — it is scattered across 50 state licensing board websites, buried in accreditation guidelines, and discussed in niche forums. No single database has centralized it. This publication exists to fill that gap.

The employer negotiation component is underappreciated. Most immigrants and career-switchers are trained to be deferential about their credentials — to explain, justify, apologize. The data shows that confidence + specific documentation + pivot to demonstrated output is significantly more effective than explanation or apology.
