## 1. a16z (Andreessen Horowitz)
*Feed: a16z.substack.com/feed | RSS name: "a16z.news"*
*Articles analyzed: "A Functional Taxonomy of World Models" (Fei-Fei Li guest post, Jun 3 2026), "Investing in Town" (Alex Rampell, Jun 3 2026)*

### Opening Style
Two distinct modes depending on author identity:
- **Guest/thought-leadership posts** (Fei-Fei Li): Opens with a philosophical epigraph ("The world is everything that is the case" — Wittgenstein), then a short punchy thesis sentence ("The world is not made of words."). Immediately establishes intellectual lineage and stakes.
- **Investment announcements** (Alex Rampell): Opens with a user-pain narrative. "For the past few years, using AI has mostly meant staring at a blank prompt box and knowing the right thing to ask it." Grounds the post in relatable experience before pivoting to the company thesis. This is the classic a16z "we invest in narratives" opener.

### Sentence Rhythm
Short, declarative sentences alternating with longer explanatory paragraphs. Heavy use of sentence fragments for emphasis: "Real help requires context and proactivity." Then a one-sentence paragraph: "Context is complicated and personal." The rhythm is almost cinematic — establishing shot, close-up, establishing shot. Fei-Fei Li is more academic-crisp, Rampell more conversational-punchy. Both share the a16z house style of short paragraphs (rarely more than 3 sentences) and generous whitespace.

### Data Handling
Light on primary data, heavy on conceptual frameworks. Fei-Fei Li's post is a taxonomy — a diagram older than the technology ("the POMDP loop") used as an organizing principle. No charts, no numbers. The "data" is the conceptual architecture itself. Rampell's post has zero quantitative data — no metrics, no TAM, no growth numbers. The evidence is narrative ("People are already leaning on Town for...") and team pedigree ("JDG was CTO at Plaid..."). This is characteristic: a16z posts sell worldviews, not spreadsheets.

### Unmistakable Voice
A curated plurality — there is no single a16z voice, but a house style that *hosts* voices. The unifying elements: (1) extreme confidence without arrogance, (2) present-tense declarations about the future ("Personal AI assistants are going to be one of the defining consumer software categories of the next decade"), (3) the mandatory legal disclaimer footer that punctures the visionary tone with compliance language. The net effect is "we are the platform for important people to say important things."

### Verbal Tics
- "That's why we're so excited by what [Company] is building." (signature investment-announcement transition)
- "This newsletter is provided for informational purposes only..." (mandatory disclaimer, always present)
- The em-dash as a parenthetical breath: "They want what AI was supposed to deliver in the first place: help."
- Capitalized Categories: "Real help requires context and proactivity."
- The "we've known [founder] for years" pedigree signal

### Personal/Research Balance
Near-zero personal voice. Even when using "we," it's institutional ("we're excited to lead Town's $55 million Series A"). Fei-Fei Li's post is highly researched but presents as authoritative synthesis, not personal investigation. The personal is intentionally suppressed in favor of the institutional. This is the opposite of a personal newsletter — it's a branded publishing operation.

### Transition Style
Abrupt paragraph breaks. No "furthermore," "moreover," "in addition." Transitions happen through juxtaposition — one paragraph ends, the next begins a new thought, and the reader is trusted to connect them. Between sections: implied by whitespace alone, no subheadings in the investment posts. Fei-Fei Li uses the classic academic "Subscribe to..." interjection, then resumes.

### Signature Move
The **problem-diagnosis → thesis → company-as-inevitable-solution** arc. This is the venture-investment narrative compressed into 600 words. The diagnosis is always broad and relatable ("Most people don't want to become prompt engineers"). The thesis is always contrarian-but-obvious-once-stated ("The winner won't be whoever ships the most features. It'll be whoever earns enough trust to hold your context"). The company is the inevitable answer. This template has been polished to mirror-finish.

## 2. Import AI (Jack Clark)
*Feed: importai.substack.com/feed | Author: Jack Clark, co-founder of Anthropic*
*Articles analyzed: "Import AI 459: AI oversight is difficult; scaling laws for protein folding models; and pricing the extinction risk of AI systems" (Jun 1 2026), "Import AI 458: Reckoning with the future; and a singularity story" (May 26 2026)*

### Opening Style
Two modes:
- **Regular issues** (#459): Opens with a one-line existential question ("Do you feel as though you are living in a revolution?"), then immediately into the standard newsletter boilerplate ("Welcome to Import AI, a newsletter about AI research. Import AI runs on arXiv, cappuccinos, and feedback from readers."). The boilerplate is a ritual — it signals continuity across a decade of weekly output.
- **Special issues** (#458): A long-form speech with a thesis in the title and an immediate structural roadmap ("This issue consists of a lengthy essay based on a speech I recently gave, and a fictional story..."). The speech itself opens with a choice framed as a binary: "explore the future, or retreat from the present."

### Sentence Rhythm
Highly varied. Jack Clark writes in three registers:
1. **The analyst**: Dense paragraphs packed with data and nested clauses. "Economists with the University of Virginia and Anthropic, and the Bank of Canada have written a paper outlining both the tremendous growth of the emerging 'AI economy' in the US, and wrestling with why this growth is hard to see in aggregate GDP statistics." Long, winding, journalistic.
2. **The confessional**: Short, intimate sentences about personal experience. "In the summer of 2023, I use AI systems to check my work for typos. By November, I am using AI to help me figure out what foods to feed my baby." A chronological litany that gains power through accumulation.
3. **The prophet**: Declarative, italicized, set apart. "AI is a tremendously powerful technology — and getting more powerful all the time." The em-dashes create a breathless quality.

He also uses the triple-asterisk `***` as a hard section break — a distinctive visual rhythm marker.

### Data Handling
Obsessive and self-aware about it. Clark literally has a subheading "Why this matters - shut up and play the Jaws theme tune:" — he knows he's about to show you data that should terrify you. He uses numbers ("growing at roughly 2,600 percent per year," "US compute spending rose from $37 billion in 2023 to $90 billion in 2024 to $219 billion in 2025") but always wraps them in interpretation. The data is never naked — it's always scaffolding for an argument. He also *generates his own data*: the newsletter itself is a data-generation engine (he built a skill to scrape his own archives and auto-generate graphs). This is uniquely meta.

### Unmistakable Voice
First-person, deeply personal, morally urgent. Jack Clark has one of the most distinctive voices in AI commentary because he combines three things that rarely coexist: (1) deep technical literacy (he reads arXiv papers for fun), (2) insider access (he's a co-founder of Anthropic), and (3) genuine existential fear dressed in calm, measured prose. He is the thoughtful person at the center of the hurricane, describing the wind speed.

### Verbal Tics
- "…" (ellipsis as a thinking pause, used as a standalone paragraph opener)
- "***" (triple asterisk section break — his visual signature)
- "Why this matters — [metaphor]:" (every data section gets an interpretive wrapper)
- "I need you to deeply feel..." (direct address, almost pleading)
- "This is not an ideal situation, but it is the one we find ourselves in." (the Clark resignation-to-reality move)
- The parenthetical aside that undercuts: "Import AI [readers - it's this newsletter!]"
- "Tell me how the world stays normal" (the rhetorical challenge, repeated)
- "Things that inspired this story:" (closing notes, like DVD commentary)
- Fiction appended to every issue ("Tech Tales") — a short speculative story

### Personal/Research Balance
Extraordinarily high on both axes. Clark is uniquely positioned to write about AI because he's both a researcher-adjacent analyst AND deeply personally entangled. Issue #458 is essentially an autobiography of his relationship with AI systems ("In January 2024, I use AI to help me understand my marriage... In August, AI persuades me to go back to therapy"). The personal IS the research — his life is the case study. This is the newsletter's superpower and why it has lasted a decade.

### Transition Style
Hard cuts. The `***` separator is a guillotine — no segue, no transition, just dead stop and new topic. Within sections, he uses subheadings formatted as colon-statements ("Why it's hard to see:", "Three ways of measuring the AI economy:", "What can we do?"). These function as handrails through dense material. Transitions between the analytical and the personal are unmarked — he'll go from GDP statistics to "How the graph feels to me" with no bridge, trusting the reader to feel the connection.

### Signature Move
The **Jaws metaphor** — building tension through data, then naming the tension with a pop-culture analogy that makes the abstract threat visceral. "In the great film Jaws there's this scene where the shark is in the water... That's what it feels like working on AI and staring at most economic data right now." He does this repeatedly: the data says normal, but my gut says shark. He then resolves with clarity: here's what to do. The structure is: data → tension → metaphor → call to action (or fiction).

## 3. NewLimit Blog (Brian Armstrong's longevity company)
*Feed: blog.newlimit.com/feed | Author: Jacob C. Kimmel (CEO & President), with Brian Armstrong as co-founder*
*Articles analyzed: "NewLimit raises $435M led by Founders Fund to bring longevity medicines to human trials" (Jun 2 2026), "January // February 2026 Progress Update" (Mar 27 2026)*

### Opening Style
**Announcement posts**: Direct, news-led. "We've closed a $435M Series C led by Founders Fund..." No throat-clearing, no narrative hook. The news IS the hook.
**Progress updates**: Temporal marker with narrative tension. "In January of 2023, we outlined a plan to discover reprogramming payloads that could make old cells look and hopefully act young. We had yet to execute our first discovery screens, build our first AI systems, or even finish assembling our lab." Immediately establishes a "look how far we've come" arc. The contrast between then (nothing) and now (breakthroughs) does the persuasive work.

### Sentence Rhythm
The progress updates use a distinctive **bullet-point as scorecard** format:
```
+1 payload that restores multiple youthful functions
0 → 20% manufacturing progress
+50 payloads that make old hepatocytes look young
2X faster payload discovery
```
This is a rhythm borrowed from startup update culture and video game achievement screens. The `+` and `→` and `X` symbols create a quantified, momentum-forward cadence. Between these scorecard items, explanatory paragraphs alternate between dense scientific detail and plain-language translation: "hepatocytes (Greek/biologist for 'liver cell')" — the parenthetical translation is a signature rhythm move.

### Data Handling
The most data-dense of all five publications. Specific metrics everywhere: "about 10^16 TF sets we might test," ">2X relative to the baseline," "4 large-scale screens," "0.5% of US GDP." Data is presented as proof-of-progress and also as a recruiting signal ("we do real science here"). The progress updates essentially function as a public lab notebook. The data handling is transparent about process ("we developed one such preclinical model") and honest about uncertainty ("it was unclear if this form of multi-functional recovery was possible").

### Unmistakable Voice
Scientist-as-builder. Jacob Kimmel writes like someone who spends his days in a lab and his nights writing up results. The voice is precise, excited-by-detail, and unpretentious. There's a palpable pride in the work that comes through without ever becoming boastful. The parenthetical translations for jargon ("Greek/biologist for 'does multiple things'") reveal a writer who genuinely wants to be understood by non-specialists. The voice is earnest, technical, and optimistic — the opposite of cynical tech commentary.

### Verbal Tics
- Parenthetical translations: "(Greek/biologist for 'liver cell')", "(Greek/biologist for 'does multiple things')"
- The `+` prefix for discoveries: "+1 payload", "+50 payloads", "+19 payloads"
- Arrow notation: "0 → 20%", "0 → 4"
- Multiplier notation: "2X faster", ">2X relative"
- "We've been privileged to share the NewLimit story in several venues recently." (humblebrag transition to media mentions)
- "We are still at the beginning of the story." (closing line, used in the funding post)
- "Thanks for reading NewLimit Blog! Subscribe for free..." (consistent sign-off, substack-native)

### Personal/Research Balance
Almost entirely research and company updates. The personal is limited to the founding story ("NewLimit was founded in 2021 by Coinbase CEO and Co-founder Brian Armstrong, former GV partner and bioengineer Blake Byers, and computational biologist Jacob Kimmel") and occasional first-person plural ("We initially believed it would take more than a decade..."). The "we" is institutional, not personal. This is a company blog, not a personal newsletter — the research IS the content.

### Transition Style
In progress updates: section breaks use thematic headers (Reprogramming medicines, Vascular program) with no transitional text. Within sections, paragraphs flow chronologically or causally: "In February, we discovered... When we began our discovery campaigns years ago... Inspired by results like these..." The transitions are temporal and logical — "because X, therefore Y." The funding announcement uses a more marketing-style flow: problem → solution → results → team → call to action.

### Signature Move
The **"then vs. now" arc** combined with the **scorecard format**. Open with where they started (nothing), present the scorecard of what's been achieved, then explain each item with enough scientific detail to be credible but enough plain language to be accessible. Close with hiring call and media mentions. This is a template optimized for three audiences simultaneously: potential investors (look at our velocity), potential hires (look at our science), and the curious public (look at our mission).

## 4. AI Futures Project (Kate Armstrong)
*Feed: aifutures.substack.com/feed | Author: Kate Armstrong (note: listed as "Daniel Kokotajlo" in task brief, but current feed shows Kate Armstrong as sole author)*
*Articles analyzed: "Pump up the Jank" (May 20 2026), "What the Fungus Knows" (Apr 16 2026)*

### Opening Style
Deliberately oblique and experiential. "Why am I captivated by the Monster Wolf robots that have been released to such fanfare in Japan? I think it's the janky aesthetics." The opening poses a personal question then answers it with an unexpected aesthetic judgment. "Sam Altman's manifesto... is a fascinating provocation relating to how we might implement AI in a way that is beneficial to society." Opens with the artifact under critique, then immediately applies pressure to its blind spots. The openings refuse summary — they start mid-thought, as if the reader has walked into a conversation already in progress.

### Sentence Rhythm
Unpredictable, digressive, associative. Armstrong writes in long, branching sentences that accumulate detail through apposition and parenthetical asides. Example: "Or the abhorrent MAGA-style girl robots that are also a core litmus test for identifying a douchebag." Then immediately pivots: "Of course we also still suffer the omnipresent influence of Kawaii when it comes to robot design but in some of the crevices of the internet you can see new approaches..." The rhythm is that of a mind unpacking itself in real time — tangents are the point, not a bug. Sentences often end with a question that reframes the preceding text.

### Data Handling
Almost no quantitative data. The "data" is cultural artifacts: Monster Wolf robots, Sam Altman's manifesto, a GrowHR robot with a specific face, the Aesthetics Fandom wiki. Evidence is presented through juxtaposition and close reading, not statistics. When she does cite something factual, it's as a throwaway ("apparently it has been available in Japan since 2020"). The analytical mode is closer to art criticism than tech analysis — the question is not "does this work?" but "what does this mean?"

### Unmistakable Voice
The artist-critic writing from inside the machine. Armstrong's voice is unmistakably that of someone who thinks about AI through the lens of aesthetics, culture, and power, rather than benchmarks and scaling laws. She is annoyed, witty, and deeply serious all at once. The tonal range is wide: from deadpan humor ("multiple types of loud") to righteous fury ("artists and cultural workers... are somehow not considered as workers, and certainly not knowledge workers") to poetic insight ("The fungus has opinions about this. It would be worth asking."). There is no one else in AI commentary writing quite like this.

### Verbal Tics
- Self-interrupting parentheticals: "(Note to readers: spend the next 12 weeks involuntarily glued within the Aesthetics Fandom wiki, no greater database has ever been built.)"
- The bracketed self-correction: "(…train? retrain? where did that guy come from anyway?)"
- The parenthetical self-reference: "(I know I keep making this point but somehow I feel like I'm still struggling to fully capture it.)"
- The extended metaphor that takes over the essay: the fungus/mycelium metaphor in "What the Fungus Knows" starts as a passing analogy and becomes the entire argumentative framework
- Rhetorical questions as section pivots: "Can we plant a flag for the janky...?"
- Pop culture references as analytical tools: Bridget Jones, Anne Hathaway in The Devil Wears Prada, Melanie Griffiths in Working Girl
- "speed sleepwalking" — the compound modifier that captures acceleration and passivity simultaneously

### Personal/Research Balance
Heavily personal. Armstrong writes in first person throughout, and her personal observations and reactions ARE the analytical method. She positions herself explicitly as an artist and cultural worker within the systems she's critiquing. The research is cultural criticism — she reads manifestos, examines robot designs, traces aesthetic lineages. There is no pretense of objectivity; the personal IS the analytical lens.

### Transition Style
Associative and digressive. Paragraphs connect through conceptual resonance rather than logical sequence. From Monster Wolf robots to MAGA-style girl robots to Kawaii to GrowHR — the thread is "robot aesthetics" but the path is wandering, exploratory. Transitions are often achieved through a single pivot word or a rhetorical question. When she wants to make a sharp turn, she uses a single-line paragraph as a hinge: "But I'm drawn to try to reframe this."

### Signature Move
The **fungus maneuver** — take an unexpected metaphor (fungus, jank, mycelium, hot tub), introduce it casually, then let it grow until it has consumed the entire argument. The metaphor starts as an illustrative aside and becomes the thesis. In "What the Fungus Knows," the fungal analogy transforms from a description of how artists are treated into a theory of what artists actually ARE — an underground network that encircles the planet, occasionally producing truffles. This is not analytical writing; it's alchemical — base observation transmuted into conceptual gold through the heat of sustained metaphorical thinking.

## Cross-Publication Observations

### The Spectrum from Institutional to Personal
These five publications fall on a clear spectrum:
- **Most institutional**: a16z (branded publishing, multiple authors, legal disclaimers) and NewLimit (company blog, progress-as-content)
- **Bridging**: Import AI (personal newsletter from an institutional insider — uniquely both)
- **Most personal**: AI Futures Project (cultural criticism from an artist's perspective)
- **Non-existent**: Simply Explained (placeholder only)

### The AI Writing About AI Phenomenon
Three of the five publications (Import AI, AI Futures, a16z) are explicitly about AI, and they reveal three fundamentally different ways to write about the same subject:
- Jack Clark: the insider-analyst, using data and personal experience as twin telescopes
- Kate Armstrong: the artist-critic, using aesthetics and culture as diagnostic tools
- a16z: the platform-investor, hosting authoritative voices to shape narratives

### Missing Voices
The task brief listed "Daniel Kokotajlo" for AI Futures Project, but the actual author is Kate Armstrong. Similarly, Brian Armstrong was listed for NewLimit but the primary voice is Jacob Kimmel. These discrepancies suggest the brief may have been based on earlier assumptions about authorship that have since changed.
