# Substack Writing Voice Taxonomy — Batch 4

Analyzed from RSS feeds and live blog content. Date: June 4, 2026.

## 1. Astral Codex Ten (Scott Alexander)

**RSS:** astralcodexten.substack.com/feed — Active, rich content

### Opening Style
Scott uses two distinct openers: (a) the **thesis-in-first-sentence** — "Constraint consequentialists believe that you should try to do good things..." or his blog tagline "P(A|B) = [P(A)*P(B|A)]/P(B), all the rest is commentary" — and (b) the **paradox/curiosity hook** — "The philosophers of the Frankfurt School practiced a technique called negative dialectics, where concepts are defined as much by what you *can't* say about them as what you *can*." He often opens with a concrete example or a puzzle that he will spend 3,000–15,000 words unwinding.

### Rhythm
**Long-form essayist.** Posts average 3,000–8,000 words. He uses short declarative paragraphs (1–3 sentences) broken by section headers (h3 level) with quirky, often literary titles: "Now The Wheels Of Heaven Stop, I Feel The Devil's Riding Crop"; "Papua New Guinea As Hot New Startup Hub." These section titles function as mini-jokes or cultural references that only land halfway through the section. He alternates between dense analytical paragraphs and single-sentence gut-punch summaries.

### Data Handling
Heavy. Scott is the most citation-dense writer in this taxonomy. He links constantly: to his own past posts ("I previously discussed..."), to Wikipedia, to academic papers, to commenters, to Twitter polls. He embeds links mid-sentence as natural parts of the argument. His characteristic move is to cite a study or poll, acknowledge its limitations, and then *use it anyway* as an intuition pump. He quantifies whenever possible but foregrounds that the numbers are approximate: "a 90% chance of ending the world, and another's has an 80% chance."

### Voice
**Bayesian rationalist / psychiatrist-philosopher / blogger-next-door.** He writes as if thinking out loud in real time. He says things like "I think this is all defensible, and right now I don't think either group is violating its particular deontological bar" — the "right now" signals provisionality. He frequently narrates his own thought process: "Here's the analogy that finally helped me," "Having only read a single book on them, I will no doubt fall into all the failure modes."

### Verbal Tics
- "I notice..." / "I don't know..." / "But I've also..."
- "Consider:" / "Suppose..." / "Maybe..."
- Self-deprecating hedging: "I would be better positioned to navigate this debate if I knew..."
- The numbered-list-as-post-body format (Open Thread 436 structure: 1:, 2:, 3:, 4:)
- Parenthetical asides that undercut his own argument
- "tbc" (to be clear)
- "the sort of X where Y" construction

### Personal/Research Ratio
**40:60 personal-to-research.** He is personally present — uses "I," discusses his internal debates within AI safety, talks about what he does as a blogger, references his own mental states. But the bulk of each post is external: history, philosophy, economics, scientific studies. He is a curator-synthesizer. His personal life appears only as context for why he's thinking about something.

### Transitions
Scott's signature transition is the **"okay, but actually"** move: he presents a simple model, demonstrates its inadequacy, then presents a slightly better model, demonstrates its inadequacy, and continues until he arrives at something adequate — or admits he hasn't. He also uses the **counterintuitive pivot**: "But this still doesn't do it for me. Consider: should I use my blog to spread misinformation if I think it would have good consequences?"

### Signature Move
**The dialectical staircase.** Write a simple formula/rule/theory. Show why it breaks on edge cases. Patch it. Show why the patch breaks. Keep climbing. By the top, the reader has internalized the complexity that makes the simple opening inadequate. Often ends with a tentative "I think" rather than a firm conclusion. This is why a Scott Alexander post on "What Deontological Bars?" opens with assassination and ends at "should I spread misinformation on my blog" — he has walked you through the entire conceptual terrain.

**Rating:** A+ — Essential. One of the most distinctive and influential voices in the rationalist/EA blogosphere. His dialectical method is the blueprint for an entire generation of bloggers.

## 2. Overcoming Bias (Robin Hanson)

**RSS:** www.overcomingbias.com/feed — Active, rich content

### Opening Style
Robin's openings are **startlingly direct and compressed.** No throat-clearing. "Consider three sources of opinions or habits:" followed by (A)(B)(C). "This is an essay. Like most of my essays, I write it mostly to strangers." He often opens with a taxonomy or a classification system that structures the entire post. When he does use a personal opening, it's disarming in its directness: "I just went to my mom's funeral, and so was reminded about my family, and of the question of what exactly one wants to do with one's life."

### Rhythm
**Dense, compressed, zero-fat prose.** Posts average 500–1,200 words but pack in more concepts than most writers' 5,000-word essays. Paragraphs are short — 2–5 sentences. No rhetorical flourish. No transitions for beauty. He stacks claim on claim. Reading Hanson feels like being fed concentrated ideas through a fire hose. There is no warmup, no conclusion paragraph that praises the reader for making it through. When the argument is done, the post ends.

### Data Handling
**Abstract concept manipulation over empirical citation.** Hanson cites his own prior work constantly (his Ph.D. on institutions, his futarchy paper, his blog posts). He occasionally cites external work — Poincare 1908, Schumpeter 1911, Ogburn 1922 — but these are almost decorative. His real data is: "I asked ChatGPT (5.5) and Claude (4.7) to give percentile estimates" or "I asked poll respondents." He treats LLMs as research assistants that can be polled for estimates. He also uses formal arrow notation for trends.

### Voice
**The alien anthropologist.** Hanson writes as if observing human behavior from outside it, using the tools of economics, evolutionary psychology, and institutional analysis to describe things most people take as given. He describes his own approach as "abstract analysis." His affect is flat, unsentimental, and radically honest about his own motivations: "I want people to say, because it's true, that I helped the world gain insight on important neglected potent topics."

### Verbal Tics
- "Alas..." (his most distinctive tic — a sigh that precedes bad news)
- "Plausibly..." (his favorite hedge — he rarely says "certainly")
- "I've said previously..." / "In my last post I reported..."
- Parenthetical citations: "(Poincare 1908, Schumpeter 1911, Ogburn 1922)"
- "Yes, even if..." (concession-then-override)
- "This seems..." / "It seems..." (epistemic modesty as default stance)
- "Consider..." as paragraph opener
- Numbered frameworks: (A)(B)(C), first/second/third
- "At my funeral, please do ask yourselves..." (unsettling directness)

### Personal/Research Ratio
**15:85 personal-to-research.** Hanson occasionally injects personal context (mom's funeral, his class background, his sons' professions) but only in service of the argument. His personal details are deployed as data points for his theories about class, status, and cultural evolution. He is not building rapport; he is demonstrating his framework's applicability to his own life.

### Transitions
Hanson's transitions are **logical rather than rhetorical.** He moves from claim to claim using explicit logical connectors: "However," "First... Second... Third...," "Thus," "Okay, then what if..." He does not use narrative transitions, metaphors, or scene-setting. The reader follows because the logical chain is tight, not because it's pleasant.

### Signature Move
**The "we are doomed but here is how we might not be" structure.** Hanson identifies a deep structural problem in human civilization (cultural drift toward forager styles, collapse of meta-institutions, decay of norms). He outlines why standard approaches won't work. He then presents 2–3 long-shot solutions, admits they are "big long-shots," and ends with "Our situation is quite desperate. And not likely to get better until a lot more people start to think about it." The combination of radical clarity about the problem, institutional thinking about solutions, and unsentimental acknowledgment of how unlikely they are to be adopted — this is Hanson's unique contribution.

**Rating:** A — Essential for the rationalist taxonomy. The purest example of "zero-warmth, maximum-insight" blogging. His posts on futarchy/decision markets, cultural drift, and meta-institutions are among the most original ideas in the blogosphere. Style is an acquired taste but unmistakably his own.

## 3. The Roots of Progress (Jason Crawford)

**RSS:** blog.rootsofprogress.org/feed.xml — Active; rootsofprogress.org/feed — institute announcements only

### Opening Style
Crawford's openings vary by format:
- **Essay**: "Let's step back and recap how we got here" (context-first). Or "Sorry I haven't replied to your email. I've been vibecoding" (personal, humorous, immediate).
- **Links digest**: Highly structured — year-end call to support, table of contents, then categorized links with commentary.
- **Historical essays** (from earlier blog): "Iron: From mythical to mundane," "Turning air into bread" — title-as-thesis, then narrative historical exposition.

His essay openings work by **lowering the stakes** — he makes big topics approachable by anchoring them in personal experience, then expanding outward.

### Rhythm
Two distinct modes:
1. **Long-form essay mode** (2,000–8,000 words): Alternates between narrative storytelling (the history of GPTs, his personal vibecoding journey) and analytical synthesis (what this means for software, for society). Paragraphs are medium-length (4–8 sentences). He uses section breaks and subheadings to organize long arguments. Excellent pacing — never feels long even at 5,000+ words.
2. **Links digest mode**: Brisk, bulleted, highly organized. Categories with bold headers. One-to-two sentence commentary per link. Feels like the world's most useful newsletter.

### Data Handling
**Moderate, example-driven.** Crawford cites specific books ("A Brief History of Intelligence" by Max Bennett), papers, blog posts, and tweets. His citations are embedded in the flow — "as I predicted" (linking to his own work), "as Dwarkesh pointed out" (linking to peers). He uses concrete examples (his todo app, his daughter's iPad game, the CSV workout analyzer) rather than statistics. He embeds charts (the METR time-horizons graph) and uses them as explanatory tools. His data handling is that of a **product manager turned writer** — specific, grounded, oriented toward what can be built.

### Voice
**Optimistic technologist / historian of progress / builder-blogger.** Crawford writes from the perspective of someone who believes deeply in human agency and technological progress. His voice is warm, accessible, and earnest — no irony, no cynicism. He is a proselytizer for "techno-humanism" but wears it lightly. When he describes vibecoding, he sounds genuinely delighted: "When I started, I would delegate a task and then do something else... But once I had 3–4 agents working in parallel... I found that I was fully occupied just reviewing their work... which is amazing."

### Verbal Tics
- "Let's step back and..." (contextualizing move)
- "OK fine, we'll try it your way, I thought" (narrating his inner dialogue)
- "Already I've built..." (demonstrating thesis through personal action)
- "I can only imagine..." (extrapolating from personal experience)
- "Hat-tip to @[user]" (generous crediting)
- "Stay tuned!" / "Back, baby!" (playful enthusiasm)
- "To oversimplify:" (then gives a clean simplification anyway)
- "This is going to change the world" (earnest prediction)

### Personal/Research Ratio
**50:50 personal-to-research.** Crawford is unusually personal for a "movement" writer. He discusses his parenting ("Daddy, why is a hundred a big number?"), his coding hobby, his reading list, his career transitions. But these personal details always serve a larger argument about progress, technology, and human agency. He is building credibility through lived experience.

### Transitions
Crawford uses **chronological and conceptual transitions.** "In parallel, the models' coding ability was growing." "The other thing that developed in parallel..." "Put all of this together..." "Stepping back..." These are the transitions of a clear-thinking engineer laying out a system architecture.

### Signature Move
**"I tried it myself, here's what happened, here's what it means."** Crawford's most distinctive pattern is the personal experiment writ large. His essay "As we may vibe" is a masterclass: personal anecdote, then historical context, then technical explanation, then personal narrative, then extrapolation to society, then back to personal. He earns his conclusions by demonstrating them in his own life first.

**Rating:** A — The most polished and accessible writer in the progress-studies movement. His personal-experiment-to-civilization-implication arc is a blueprint for how to make abstract ideas feel concrete and urgent.

## 4. Vitalik Buterin (vitalik.ca)

**RSS:** vitalik.eth.limo/feed.xml (actual blog); vitalik.substack.com/feed (placeholder only)

### Opening Style
Vitalik's openings are **technical and declarative.** He tends to state the topic and its importance in the first 1–2 sentences, then immediately begin explaining. There is no throat-clearing, no anecdotal hook. His blog is closer to an academic paper or a technical specification than to a personal blog. Example titles: "A shallow dive into formal verification," "Memory access is O(N^[1/3])," "Exploring circle STARKs" — these could be conference paper titles.

### Rhythm
**Variable but technical.** Vitalik runs two parallel modes:
1. **Technical explainer mode** (e.g., "A GKR Tutorial," "Binius: highly efficient proofs over binary fields"): Dense, equation-heavy, structured like a textbook chapter. Builds from first principles.
2. **Philosophical/policy mode** (e.g., "My techno-optimism," "Make Ethereum Cypherpunk Again," "Balance of power"): More discursive, still highly structured, uses frameworks and taxonomies. Paragraphs are medium-length.

His posting frequency is remarkable — roughly 2–3 posts/month sustained over 8+ years (2017–2026), covering cryptography, mechanism design, governance, political philosophy, AI, travel, and personal reflection. Total posts in feed: ~150+.

### Data Handling
**Math-heavy when technical, concept-heavy when philosophical.** In technical posts, Vitalik writes actual mathematics with formal notation. In philosophical posts, he builds conceptual frameworks: the bulldozer/vetocracy axis, convex vs. concave dispositions, the tree ring model of culture and politics. He rarely cites external authorities — he builds arguments from first principles. When he does cite, it's to blog posts, papers, or his own prior work. He uses his own life as a data source: "My self-sovereign / local / private / secure LLM setup" is literally him documenting his own setup.

### Voice
**The philosopher-engineer.** Vitalik writes as someone who thinks through problems from scratch. His voice is earnest, intellectually honest, and surprisingly humble for someone of his stature. He changes his mind publicly: "Why I used to prefer permissive licenses and now favor copyleft." He writes about coordination failures, public goods, and governance with the same analytical rigor he applies to zero-knowledge proofs. There is no snark, no hot takes, no engagement bait.

### Verbal Tics
- First-principles framing: "What even is an institution?"
- Coinage of memorable conceptual frameworks: "d/acc," "degen communism," "soulbound," "The Three Transitions"
- "I support it only if it's open source" — strong, clear normative positions
- "My response to..." — engagement with other thinkers' ideas
- Translations: publishes in multiple languages (Chinese, Spanish, Turkish, French) — unique among this group
- April 1 posts that are high-effort intellectual jokes

### Personal/Research Ratio
**20:80 personal-to-research/technical.** Vitalik occasionally writes personal posts ("The end of my childhood," "My 40-liter backpack travel guide") but the vast majority of his output is technical or philosophical. Even his personal posts are analytical — "The end of my childhood" is about Ethereum's maturation through a personal lens.

### Transitions
Vitalik uses **logical sequencing.** He numbers things. He uses "First... Second... Third..." He structures posts as linked conceptual chains. There is a clear architectural quality to his writing — you can see the scaffolding.

### Signature Move
**Naming things.** Vitalik's greatest contribution to intellectual discourse is his ability to coin frameworks that become part of the lexicon: "d/acc," "The Three Transitions," "soulbound tokens," "the bulldozer/vetocracy axis," "convex vs. concave dispositions," "the tree ring model of culture and politics." Each is a compact conceptual tool that readers can take away and apply.

**Rating:** A — Unique in the taxonomy. No other writer spans technical cryptography tutorials and political philosophy with equal fluency. The framework-naming habit has had more impact on Silicon Valley discourse than most writers' entire bodies of work.

## 5. cdixon (Chris Dixon)

**RSS:** cdixon.substack.com/feed — Active, medium content volume

### Opening Style
Dixon opens with **positioning statements.** "It's fashionable right now to declare that 'non-financial use cases of crypto are dead.'" He identifies a prevailing narrative, then pushes against it. His openings establish: (1) what people are saying, (2) why they're wrong or incomplete, (3) what he's going to argue instead. This is the classic **"contrarian thesis"** opener.

### Rhythm
**Short, punchy, VC-blog style.** Posts average 500–1,500 words. Paragraphs are 2–4 sentences. He uses section headers (h3 level) to break arguments into digestible chunks: "The order of operations matters," "Policy as the missing piece," "What the long game looks like." The rhythm is designed for skimming — you can get the argument from headers alone, then dive into paragraphs for detail. Very much the a16z house style.

### Data Handling
**Light on data, heavy on framework and narrative.** Dixon rarely cites statistics or studies. His evidence comes from: (a) his own investment experience (Coinbase, Maker, Compound, Uniswap, Morpho), (b) historical analogies (the internet's packet-switching era, neural networks dating to 1943, the internet from the 1960s), (c) named frameworks he's developed ("come for the tool, stay for the network," "The next big thing starts out looking like a toy"). He is a **framework-builder** first, empiricist second.

### Voice
**Venture capitalist as technology historian.** Dixon writes with the confidence of someone who has bet billions on his thesis. He uses "we" to refer to a16z: "We play the long game at a16z and a16z crypto." The voice is measured, authoritative, and strategic — never panicked, never breathless. He is playing a "long game" and his writing reflects that: no hot takes, no reacting to daily price movements.

### Verbal Tics
- "It's fashionable right now to declare..." (contrarian positioning)
- "We are clearly in..." / "We expected..." (insider perspective)
- "Big things take time." (aphoristic summarization)
- "The [adjective] years are what make the [adjective] years possible." (parallel structure)
- "I believe..." / "My thesis is..." (owning the argument)
- bullet-pointed framework lists
- Signed "Chris" at the end of personal-feeling posts

### Personal/Research Ratio
**10:90 personal-to-research.** Dixon is rarely personal. He signs posts "Chris" and occasionally mentions travel or speaking, but his writing is almost entirely thesis-driven.

### Transitions
Dixon uses **thematic headers** as primary transitions. Between sections, he uses summary-then-forward transitions. Within sections, he uses direct logical connectors.

### Signature Move
**The three-era framework.** Dixon's defining intellectual contribution is periodizing technology into eras: read -> write -> own. He organizes complex technological change into clean, memorable phases that imply inevitable forward motion. Combined with his aphoristic style ("The messy years are what make the obvious years possible"), this makes his writing exceptionally quotable.

**Rating:** B+ — Clear, influential, but narrow. His Substack is primarily a thesis-articulation vehicle. The frameworks are powerful and have shaped how an entire industry talks about itself.

## Cross-Writer Comparative Analysis

### The Rationalist Axis
Scott Alexander and Robin Hanson share rationalist heritage but diverge dramatically:
- **Scott**: warm, self-deprecating, long-form, narrative-driven, builds rapport through vulnerability
- **Robin**: cool, unsentimental, compressed, argument-driven, builds logical chains without emotional handrails
- **Both**: highly original, skeptical of consensus, willing to follow arguments to uncomfortable conclusions

### The Techno-Optimist Axis
Jason Crawford and Chris Dixon both advocate for technological progress:
- **Crawford**: personal, granular, "I tried it" mode, movement-builder, synthesizer-historian
- **Dixon**: strategic, high-level, "we invested" mode, capital allocator, framework-builder
- **Both**: use historical analogies, believe in "long games," structure arguments around eras/phases

### The Polymath Outlier
Vitalik Buterin is in a category of his own: a technical founder who writes at the intersection of cryptography, mechanism design, governance, and philosophy. His closest comparator might be a less literary Paul Graham or a more technical Scott Alexander.

### Voice Spectrum (Warm to Cool)
Crawford (warmest) -> Scott Alexander -> Vitalik -> Dixon -> Hanson (coolest)

### Post Length Spectrum (Shortest to Longest)
Hanson (~800 words) -> Dixon (~1,000) -> Crawford (~2,500) -> Vitalik (~3,000) -> Scott Alexander (~5,000+)

### Data Density Spectrum (Lightest to Heaviest)
Dixon -> Crawford -> Hanson -> Vitalik -> Scott Alexander
