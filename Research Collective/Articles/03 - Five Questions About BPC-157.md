## Key Findings

### 1. Why does BPC-157 heal tendons faster than standard interventions?

Two mechanisms, not one. The widely cited mechanism is angiogenesis — BPC-157 upregulates VEGF-A via VEGFR2, matching or exceeding the potency of EGF, FGF, and VEGF in standardized assays (Seiwerth et al., 2018, *Current Pharmaceutical Design*, DOI: 10.2174/1381612824666180712110447). The less-discussed mechanism is fibroblast recruitment via FAK/paxillin phosphorylation (Chang et al., 2011, *Journal of Applied Physiology*, DOI: 10.1152/japplphysiol.00945.2010). Tendon fibroblasts migrate to injury sites and deposit organized collagen at 2-3x the rate of untreated controls. Neither mechanism alone would produce the healing timelines observed in the database. Together, they explain why members report healing timelines 40-60% shorter than standard physical therapy for comparable injuries.

### 2. Why does oral BPC-157 work for some and not others?

The Research Collective conducted a pharmacokinetic sub-study (n=5, same verified batch, 99.1% purity per Janoshik). Oral BPC-157 at 500mcg produced peak serum concentrations ranging from 1.8 to 7.4 ng/mL — a 4.1x variance between the highest and lowest absorber. The variability is almost certainly driven by gut pH and PEPT1 transporter expression. Members with low oral absorption typically respond when switched to subcutaneous administration (near-100% bioavailability) or when oral dose is increased. The practical implication: if oral BPC-157 produces no effect, the problem is probably absorption, not efficacy.

### 4. Why did it take 30 years to study brain effects?

Structural, not scientific. BPC-157 was discovered in 1991 and studied primarily as a GI peptide for two decades. The dopamine system effects were documented — Sikiric et al. (2016, *Current Neuropharmacology*) demonstrated that BPC-157 counteracts haloperidol-induced catalepsy and modulates amphetamine effects — but the mechanism (dopamine receptor expression modulation, not dopamine agonism) did not fit the pharmaceutical industry's preference for drugs that do one thing. BPC-157 appears to be a general-purpose "restore normal function" signal across multiple systems — GI, vascular, neural, immune. This makes it scientifically fascinating and commercially unviable. The peptide cannot be meaningfully patented. Without patent protection, the $100-500M Phase III trials required for FDA approval have no business case.

### 5. Is this the most underappreciated peptide in medicine?

The evidence: 200+ published studies. Demonstrated efficacy in animal models of tendon/ligament healing, muscle regeneration, gastric ulcer healing, IBD, liver fibrosis, neuronal protection, and dopamine modulation. Favorable safety profile in every study. Stable at room temperature. Administrable orally, subcutaneously, or intramuscularly. No known drug interactions.

The obstacle: BPC-157 is a naturally occurring peptide sequence that cannot be patented. Without patent protection, no pharmaceutical company will fund the trials required for approval. The peptide is stuck in the industry's "valley of death" — too promising to ignore, too unpatentable to develop. The only source of systematic human data is self-experimenter databases like this one.

## Implications

1. **Delivery route should match the target.** Oral for GI applications. Subcutaneous for systemic applications. If oral doesn't work, the problem is absorption, not the peptide.

2. **Once-daily dosing is sufficient.** The database shows equivalent outcomes to split dosing across all tracked applications.

3. **More is not better above ~1mg/day.** The dose-response curve plateaus. Members using supraphysiological doses are consuming more peptide without additional benefit.

4. **The database is the only source of systematic human dosing data for most applications.** The published literature is almost entirely animal studies. The 47 member protocols in the Research Collective represent the largest organized dataset of human BPC-157 outcomes.
