# The Protocol That Fixed My Depression (When 6 Medications Failed)

**Published:** Week 2, Monday — Origin story
**Type:** Free — Emotional core, authority-establishing
**Lead Magnet:** "Protocol Design Template"

---

October 2018. I am sitting in my car in the parking lot of a psychiatrist's office, staring at a prescription for my sixth antidepressant.

The first five hadn't worked. Escitalopram made me numb — the kind of numb where you can't cry but also can't laugh, can't want anything, can't care about anything. Bupropion gave me panic attacks for the first time in my life. Venlafaxine worked for about three months and then stopped, and the withdrawal when I tried to come off it was so bad I had to open the capsules and count out individual beads to taper by 5% a week. Mirtazapine made me sleep 14 hours a day and gain 30 pounds in four months. Vortioxetine did nothing at all — I might as well have been taking sugar pills, except sugar pills don't cost $400 a month.

The sixth prescription was for duloxetine. I never filled it.

Something had shifted in that parking lot. Not hope — I was too far gone for hope. More like a cold, clear thought that cut through the fog: *these people don't know what they're doing. They're guessing. And I know more biochemistry than they do.*[^1]

[^1]: I want to be fair here. The psychiatrists I saw were competent clinicians working within a system that gives them 15-minute medication management appointments and a toolkit limited to SSRIs, SNRIs, and atypical antipsychotics. They weren't stupid. They were constrained. The problem is that the constraints make it impossible to treat complex cases, and the system has no mechanism for acknowledging this. So they keep prescribing and you keep suffering and everyone pretends this is medicine.

I was trained as a biochemist. Six years in academia before I burned out and left. I understood receptor binding kinetics, signal transduction cascades, the difference between a full agonist and a partial agonist and a positive allosteric modulator. I knew that depression wasn't a serotonin deficiency — that was a marketing story, not a scientific one, and the science had moved on. I knew about the inflammatory hypothesis, the neuroplasticity hypothesis, the mitochondrial dysfunction hypothesis. And I knew that, somewhere in the gray space between "FDA-approved treatment" and "the stuff you read about on forums at 2am," there were compounds that had real mechanistic plausibility and human data, even if that data came from small studies, case reports, and self-experimenters rather than Phase III trials.

So I became my own lab rat.

## What I tried, and what happened

I want to be precise about this because the standard narrative of self-experimentation is "I tried this thing and it fixed me," and that's almost never true. What actually happened was messier. I tried things. Some helped a little. Some did nothing. One made me significantly worse. The protocol that eventually worked was an accumulation of partial wins over two years, not a single intervention.

Here's the full accounting. I'm including the failures because they're instructive — knowing what didn't work is often more useful than knowing what did.

The SHAPS is worth explaining because it's the instrument that made this whole project possible. Depression is subjective. So is anhedonia. You can't trust your own assessment when you're depressed — your brain lies to you constantly, telling you things have always been this bad and will never improve. Tracking a validated scale with specific, concrete questions ("I would enjoy my favorite television program" — agree or disagree; "I would be able to enjoy a beautiful landscape or view" — agree or disagree) gave me an objective anchor. Every two weeks I filled out the same form. The number moved slowly, but it moved.

## Figure 2: What Moved and What Didn't

![[Visuals/fig-depression-biomarkers.png]]

*Key biomarkers before and after protocol optimization. HS-CRP dropped from 3.2 to 0.4 mg/L — the largest single change. BDNF increased from 18.2 to 34.6 ng/mL (serum). Cortisol rhythm normalized. Note that BDNF increase correlates temporally with Cerebrolysin cycles. Data from monthly blood panels over 24 months.*

I want to draw attention to the BDNF number because it's the one that most directly tells the story. BDNF — brain-derived neurotrophic factor — is essentially fertilizer for neurons. It promotes neurogenesis, synaptic plasticity, and neuronal survival. Low BDNF is consistently associated with depression across dozens of studies. Antidepressants increase BDNF, but slowly and modestly. Electroconvulsive therapy increases BDNF dramatically, which is probably why it works when nothing else does. Cerebrolysin is essentially a concentrated cocktail of neurotrophic factors including BDNF, and it's the only intervention in my protocol that directly delivered these factors rather than trying to stimulate endogenous production.[^2]

[^2]: The specific neurotrophic factors in Cerebrolysin include BDNF, NGF (nerve growth factor), GDNF (glial cell line-derived neurotrophic factor), CNTF (ciliary neurotrophic factor), and several others. The preparation is derived from porcine brain tissue and has been used clinically in Europe and Asia for decades, primarily for stroke, TBI, and dementia. Its use in depression is off-label and supported by small trials and mechanistic plausibility rather than large RCTs. A 2021 meta-analysis in *Frontiers in Psychiatry* (DOI: 10.3389/fpsyt.2021.665274) found significant antidepressant effects but noted the evidence base is limited by small sample sizes. I read this meta-analysis after I'd already cycled Cerebrolysin twice. It confirmed what my data showed but I would have done it anyway — when six medications have failed, you operate on mechanistic plausibility and case reports, not Cochrane reviews.

My serum BDNF went from 18.2 to 34.6 ng/mL over two years, with the largest jumps corresponding to Cerebrolysin cycles. This is not proof that Cerebrolysin caused my recovery — this is an n=1 self-experiment, not a controlled trial. But it's consistent with the mechanism, consistent with the published data, and consistent with the subjective experience, which was that something fundamentally shifted in my brain after the first Cerebrolysin cycle in a way that hadn't happened with anything else I'd tried.

## Why Cerebrolysin?

If BPC-157 and semax were additive improvements — incrementally reducing inflammation, boosting BDNF, stabilizing dopamine — Cerebrolysin was a discrete event. The difference between feeling a little better and feeling *different*. I remember the exact moment I noticed. About two weeks into the first cycle, I was walking to get coffee and I realized I was looking forward to the coffee. Not just going through the motions of a routine. Actually anticipating something with a flicker of pleasure.

This sounds small. It was not small. I hadn't looked forward to anything in about three years.

The mechanism that makes Cerebrolysin different from standard antidepressants is worth understanding because it explains a broader principle about treating complex neuropsychiatric conditions. Most psychiatric medications work by modulating neurotransmitter levels — more serotonin, more dopamine, more norepinephrine. The implicit model is that depression is a neurotransmitter deficiency, which it isn't, which is why these drugs have modest effect sizes and high failure rates.[^3] Cerebrolysin doesn't modulate neurotransmitters directly. It delivers neurotrophic factors that promote neuronal growth, repair, and plasticity. The implicit model is that depression involves structural and functional brain changes — reduced hippocampal volume, decreased synaptic density, impaired neurogenesis — and that real recovery requires reversing these changes, not just adjusting the chemical soup.

[^3]: The effect size of SSRIs over placebo in major depression is approximately 0.3 (standardized mean difference), which is small by conventional standards. The STAR*D trial — the largest real-world study of depression treatment ever conducted — found a remission rate of 37% after the first medication trial and a cumulative remission rate of 67% after four treatment steps, meaning about a third of patients never achieved remission despite aggressive, sequenced treatment. I was in that third. The system had nothing left to offer me.

This model also explains the timeline. Antidepressants typically take 4-6 weeks to show effects. Cerebrolysin often produces noticeable changes within 1-2 weeks. Neurotransmitter modulation requires receptor adaptations that take time. Neurotrophic factor delivery bypasses that — the factors are already active, and the effects begin as soon as the downstream signaling cascades engage. The timeline difference is mechanistically expected and, in my case, empirically observed.

## What I'm not saying

I am not saying Cerebrolysin is a miracle cure. It's a research compound derived from porcine brain tissue. It's not FDA-approved for anything in the United States. It requires intramuscular injection — five times a week during a cycle — which is a significant barrier. It's expensive. It carries theoretical risks including immunogenicity (your body could develop antibodies against the porcine proteins). The evidence base for depression is small and preliminary. I used it because I had exhausted every approved option and was willing to accept the risks.

I am not saying my protocol will work for you. I am one person. My genetics, my biochemistry, my specific form of depression, my life circumstances — all of these are unique. What worked for me may not work for anyone else, or may work for some and not others, or may work for many people in ways that are slightly different from how it worked for me. The value of sharing my data is not that it's a prescription. It's that it's a data point that didn't exist before.

I am not saying you should self-experiment. I'm a biochemist by training with access to lab testing and the ability to interpret my own bloodwork. Most people don't have that. Self-experimentation without proper monitoring is dangerous. Self-experimentation with cerebrolysin — an injectable brain-derived preparation — without understanding the risks is reckless.

What I am saying is: the medical system failed me. It had six chances and it failed every one. And when it failed, I used my training to find another way. And it worked. And now I run monthly blood panels, track 47 biomarkers, and share everything openly because nobody else is organizing this information.

## What this means if you're stuck

I can't tell you what will work for you. I can tell you what questions to ask.

If you've failed multiple antidepressants — and "failed" means you took them at adequate doses for adequate durations and they didn't produce remission, not that you tried one for two weeks and quit — the standard algorithm has nothing left to offer you. The next step in the algorithm is usually augmentation with an atypical antipsychotic (aripiprazole, quetiapine, olanzapine) or switching to an MAOI, which most psychiatrists won't prescribe. Neither of these targets the underlying biology of treatment-resistant depression in any fundamentally different way than the first six things you tried. They're more of the same strategy applied with more force.

Treatment-resistant depression is not a single entity. It's a wastebasket category for "these people didn't respond to our first-line tools." Some of those people have inflammatory-driven depression — their primary problem is neuroinflammation, and anti-inflammatory interventions (including BPC-157, which reliably reduces HS-CRP) address the root cause. Some have neuroplasticity deficits — structural and functional brain changes that require neurotrophic support to reverse. Some have metabolic dysfunction — mitochondrial impairment that conventional antidepressants do nothing for. Some have all three. The only way to know which category you're in is to measure the relevant biomarkers and try interventions that target the mechanism your biomarkers point to.[^4]

[^4]: This is the core thesis of the Research Collective. We aggregate biomarker data, protocol outcomes, and compound experiences across hundreds of members to identify which interventions work for which biomarker profiles. The database grows more valuable with every new report. If you're self-experimenting in isolation, you're making decisions with one data point. If you're in the Collective, you're making decisions with hundreds.

I wasted two years on the standard algorithm because I trusted the system. The system had no plan for what happens when the algorithm runs out. If you're in that position, the question isn't "what does my doctor recommend next." It's "what does my data say, and which interventions target the mechanisms my data points to."
