## Pattern 1: "Normal" means almost nothing

The single most consistent finding across 200+ panels is the gap between "normal" and "optimal." This is not a small gap. It's the difference between a population that includes sick people and a population that doesn't.

![[Visuals/fig-normal-vs-optimal.png]]

*Percentage of new members whose labs fall within optimal range vs. standard reference range. Across 12 markers, the average member has only 3-4 markers at optimal levels despite being told their labs are "normal." Most striking: only 12% of members have optimal magnesium RBC, 15% have optimal fasting insulin, and 18% have optimal vitamin D.*

Look at the insulin number. 68% of members have fasting insulin in the standard reference range — under 25 µIU/mL. Only 15% are in the optimal range — under 5 µIU/mL. That means 53% of members have "normal" insulin that's actually 2-5x the optimal level. These are people whose doctors told them their metabolic health was fine, who are walking around with insulin resistance that won't show up on their glucose tests for another 5-10 years.

The magnesium RBC number is even worse. 90% of members have serum magnesium in the standard range. Only 12% have optimal RBC magnesium. The body maintains blood magnesium at all costs, pulling it from bones and tissues. Your serum test can be textbook while your cells are running at partial capacity across 300+ enzymatic reactions that use magnesium as a cofactor. 88% of members have evidence of cellular magnesium depletion that their standard labs would never catch.

This is not a sampling artifact. This is what happens when you test an optimized population against optimal ranges. The average person walking around with "normal" bloodwork is not fine. They're slowly deteriorating in ways that won't become clinically obvious until the damage has accumulated for years.

## Figure 2: Which Improvements Actually Predict Success?

Not all biomarker improvements are equal. Some matter for how you feel. Some don't.

![[Visuals/fig-biomarker-predictors.png]]

*Percentage of members who achieved their primary outcome goal among those who normalized each marker. HS-CRP normalization is the strongest predictor: 72% of members who brought HS-CRP into optimal range achieved their protocol goals. Magnesium RBC improvement is the weakest predictor at 32%.*

HS-CRP normalization is the strongest single predictor of protocol success in our dataset. Members who reduced their HS-CRP from above 1.0 to below 0.5 mg/L were more than twice as likely to achieve their primary outcome compared to members whose HS-CRP didn't move. This makes mechanistic sense — systemic inflammation is upstream of almost everything. It impairs insulin sensitivity, suppresses thyroid function, disrupts neurotransmitter synthesis, and interferes with sleep architecture. Fixing inflammation doesn't just reduce one number. It creates the conditions for everything else to improve.

The weaker predictors are informative in the opposite direction. Magnesium RBC improvement alone — without concurrent improvements in inflammation or insulin — predicts very little. Members who raised their magnesium from 4 to 6 mg/dL but didn't address their HS-CRP or fasting insulin generally reported minimal subjective improvement. This is not to say magnesium doesn't matter. It does. But magnesium repletion in the context of ongoing inflammation is like filling a leaky bucket. Fix the inflammation first, then replete. The order matters.

## Figure 3: How Long Does This Actually Take?

The second most common question from new members, after "what should I test," is "how long until I feel better?" Here's the data.

![[Visuals/fig-time-to-optimal.png]]

*Median months to reach optimal range for each marker, with interquartile ranges. HS-CRP takes 6 months (IQR 4-10). Fasting insulin takes 8 months (IQR 5-14). Vitamin D and homocysteine are fastest: 4 months median. The full optimization of all 12 markers typically takes 2-3 years.*

Three observations from this chart.

First, the timeline is longer than most people expect. The biohacking internet is full of stories about people who "fixed their labs in 30 days." Those stories are either about people who started from a much better baseline than our average member, or they're cherry-picked outliers, or they're exaggerating. Real, sustained biomarker optimization across multiple systems takes months to years, not weeks.

Second, the markers that respond fastest — vitamin D and homocysteine — are the ones with the most straightforward interventions. Take vitamin D, levels go up. Take methylated B vitamins if you have MTHFR, homocysteine goes down. The markers that respond slowest — fasting insulin, HbA1c, magnesium RBC — are the ones that require systemic changes. You can't fix insulin resistance with a supplement. You have to change your diet, your exercise, your sleep, your stress levels. The body resists these changes because they require it to fundamentally alter its metabolic set points.

Third, the interquartile ranges are wide. The fastest quartile of members optimizes their HS-CRP in 4 months. The slowest quartile takes 10 months. Same intervention, different people, 2.5x difference in timeline. This is why tracking biomarkers matters. If you're a slow responder and you're comparing yourself to a fast responder, you'll conclude the protocol isn't working and quit right before it would have started helping.

## Figure 4: The Most Common Mistakes in Reading Your Own Labs

This is the chart that makes me want to grab doctors by the shoulders and shake them.

![[Visuals/fig-missed-patterns.png]]

*The six most common patterns missed by standard lab interpretation. Percentages show what fraction of new member panels show each pattern. Over half of new members have elevated HS-CRP with normal standard CRP. Over half have elevated fasting insulin with normal glucose. These are not rare edge cases — they are the majority of people who have been told their labs are fine.*

Look at the top two. 58% of members have HS-CRP above 2.0 mg/L while their standard CRP is flagged normal by the lab. 52% have fasting insulin above 10 µIU/mL while their fasting glucose is below 100 mg/dL. More than half of people walking around with "normal labs" have either systemic inflammation or insulin resistance — or both — that their standard panels can't detect.

The free testosterone pattern is the one that makes men angriest when they discover it. 38% of male members have free testosterone below 10 ng/dL while their total testosterone is above 400 ng/dL — squarely in the "normal" range. They've been told their hormones are fine, sometimes by multiple doctors, while experiencing symptoms consistent with hypogonadism. The problem isn't their testosterone production. It's that their SHBG is elevated — usually from the insulin resistance that their standard labs also didn't catch.

The reverse T3 pattern is the sleeper. 34% of members have reverse T3 above 15 ng/dL with TSH in the standard 0.5-4.5 range. Nobody checks reverse T3 on a standard panel. These members are functionally hypothyroid — their active T3 can't bind because rT3 is occupying the receptors — and their doctors keep telling them their thyroid is fine. TSH is a pituitary hormone. It tells you what your brain is asking for, not what your thyroid is delivering. The fact that this is still the standard screening test in 2026 is, in my professional opinion, ridiculous.

## What this means if you're looking at your own labs

The most important lesson from 200 panels is not about any specific marker. It's about how to read a lab report.

When you get your results back, the lab will highlight values outside the reference range. Those highlights exist to flag acute pathology — values that suggest you might be actively sick. They are not designed to flag suboptimal function. A value can be well within the reference range and still be contributing to your symptoms, your brain fog, your fatigue, your poor recovery. The reference range tells you whether you might need immediate medical attention. The optimal range tells you whether your biology is running at full capacity.

The second lesson is that biomarkers travel in clusters. You rarely have just one thing wrong. Elevated HS-CRP drives insulin resistance. Insulin resistance drives SHBG up, which tanks free testosterone. Inflammation impairs thyroid conversion, raising reverse T3. The pattern is consistent across panels. Fixing one marker without addressing the upstream drivers is like treating symptoms without treating the disease.

The third lesson is what I keep coming back to: the standard medical system is not designed for optimization. It's designed for disease detection. The tools it gives you — standard panels, reference ranges, annual physicals — are calibrated for a population that expects to get sick eventually and wants to catch it early. They are not calibrated for a population that wants to operate at full capacity for as long as possible. If that's what you want, you need different tools.
