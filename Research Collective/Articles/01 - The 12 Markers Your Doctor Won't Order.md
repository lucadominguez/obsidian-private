# What Your Bloodwork Is Actually Telling You (And What It Isn't)

**Published:** Week 1, Monday — Flagship article
**Type:** Free — Authority-building, shareable
**Lead Magnet:** "12 Lab Markers Checklist PDF"

---

A few years ago I added up how much I'd spent on bloodwork and the number was $15,000.

This is not a flex. This is embarrassing. It means I spent fifteen thousand dollars before I understood what I was looking at. For two years I was pulling monthly panels, 47 markers each time, and reading them completely wrong. I'd get the results back, scan for the red flags the lab had helpfully highlighted, see nothing, and think: alright, I'm fine.

I was not fine.

I was gaining weight I couldn't lose. My brain was working at maybe 70% of what it used to. I was sliding into a depression that eventually burned through six different antidepressants without responding to any of them. My doctor kept telling me my labs were normal.

The problem — and I want to be precise about this because it took me an embarrassingly long time to figure it out — is what "normal" actually means on a lab report. Those reference ranges? The ones with the little brackets showing where you fall? They're not based on what's healthy. They're based on what's typical. Specifically, they're the middle 95% of whatever population that particular lab happens to test. A population that includes diabetics. People with metabolic syndrome. People walking around with systemic inflammation they don't know about yet. Being average in a population that's getting sicker every year is not the achievement it sounds like.[^1]

[^1]: There's an entire subfield of laboratory medicine devoted to this problem — it's called "reference interval establishment" and it's depressing reading. Labs are supposed to validate their ranges against healthy populations but in practice most just adopt whatever ranges came with the analyzer they bought from Roche or Abbott. If you want to go deep on this, the CLSI EP28-A3C guideline is the standard document. I've read it. It did not make me happier.

The 12 markers below are the ones that actually moved for me, and the ones I now check in every new member who joins the Research Collective. For each one, I've included where I started, where I ended up, and what I did to get there. I'm not a doctor. I'm a biochemist who spent six years in academia before realizing the compounds that actually change lives were never going to make it through FDA approval — the incentives point the wrong direction and the timeline is measured in decades. So I started experimenting on myself and tracking everything. What follows is data, not medical advice.

## 1. HS-CRP

There are two CRP tests. The regular one flags acute infection — your body mounting a full-scale immune response to something. The high-sensitivity version measures the low-grade background inflammation that doesn't set off any alarms but is quietly damaging your blood vessels, your brain, and your metabolic machinery year after year.

My regular CRP was normal. My HS-CRP was 3.2 mg/L — above the 3.0 threshold that stratifies "high cardiovascular risk" in the Reynolds Risk Score, a risk model that outperforms the standard Framingham score specifically because it includes HS-CRP. The American Heart Association added HS-CRP to its prevention guidelines in 2018 for a reason.

Let me put that number in context. Cardiovascular risk stratification uses these cutoffs: under 1.0 is low risk, 1.0 to 3.0 is average risk, above 3.0 is high risk. For optimal function — and I mean actually optimal, not "you probably won't have a heart attack this year" — you want it under 0.5. Mine is now 0.4.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HS-CRP | <3.0 mg/L | <0.5 mg/L | 3.2 | 0.4 |

The weird thing about HS-CRP is you don't feel it. At 3.2 I felt... normal. A little tired maybe, but who isn't? The inflammation had been there so long I'd forgotten what baseline felt like. It took about six months of systematic hunting to get it down — cleaning up my diet, fixing my insulin (more on that in a minute), identifying a few food sensitivities I didn't know I had. None of this was dramatic. It was just... methodical. The kind of boring, incremental work that doesn't make for good before-and-after photos but actually changes your long-term trajectory.

This is a theme you're going to notice: the most important interventions are usually the least exciting ones.

## 2. Homocysteine

Homocysteine is an amino acid that accumulates when your methylation cycle isn't working properly. Elevated levels are one of the strongest predictors of cognitive decline we have — stronger than cholesterol is for heart disease, in some analyses.[^2] And almost nobody checks it.

[^2]: The definitive reference here is the 2002 Smith paper in *Neurology* showing that homocysteine >14 µmol/L doubled Alzheimer's risk, but the more practically useful finding is that risk starts climbing well before you hit the "high" cutoff. There's a 2018 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Alzheimer's Disease* that's worth reading if you want the full picture.

My first reading was 12.1 µmol/L. The lab said normal — their reference range goes up to 15. Optimal is under 7.

Getting from 12.1 to 6.8 was straightforward once I understood the mechanism: I have an MTHFR mutation[^3] that makes me bad at converting regular B vitamins into their active methylated forms. So I was taking a B-complex that my body couldn't actually use. Switching to methylfolate (5-MTHF, 1mg daily) and methyl-B12 (methylcobalamin, 1,000mcg daily) brought it down over about three months. For comparison, standard folic acid supplementation typically reduces homocysteine by only 10-15%. Methylated forms reduce it by 25-45% in MTHFR carriers. Same vitamins, different chemical form. That was it.

[^3]: Yes, that's the real acronym. Geneticists are either oblivious or they know exactly what they're doing. MTHFR codes for methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase, the enzyme that converts folate into its active form. About 40% of people have at least one copy of the C677T variant that reduces enzyme efficiency.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Homocysteine | <15 µmol/L | <7 µmol/L | 12.1 | 6.8 |

## 3. Free Testosterone

Total testosterone is a decoy number.

My total was 520 ng/dL — right in the middle of the reference range. Textbook. My free testosterone — the fraction that's not bound to SHBG and can actually enter your cells — was 8.2 ng/dL, well below the 15 ng/dL threshold where people start experiencing symptoms.

The mechanism here is worth understanding because it explains why so many men get told their hormones are "fine" when they're functionally hypogonadal. SHBG — sex hormone binding globulin — is a protein that grabs testosterone and makes it biologically inert. The more SHBG you have, the less of your testosterone is actually usable, regardless of how much you're producing. Insulin resistance drives SHBG up. So does chronic inflammation. So does aging. So you can have perfectly adequate testosterone production and still be functionally deficient because too much of it is tied up.

I lowered my SHBG by fixing my insulin (marker #6) and reducing inflammation (marker #1). Boron supplementation helped — there's decent evidence that 10mg/day of boron can lower SHBG by around 25%.[^4] Free T went from 8.2 to 16.1 over about eight months. Total barely moved — 520 to 580. If I'd only been tracking total T, I would have concluded that nothing was working.

[^4]: Naghii et al., 2011, *Journal of Trace Elements in Medicine and Biology*. Small study but the effect size was large and the mechanism is plausible. I'll take what I can get.

| Marker | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total T | 500-900 ng/dL | 520 | 580 |
| Free T | >15 ng/dL | 8.2 | 16.1 |

## 4. Vitamin D (25-Hydroxy)

There are two Vitamin D tests and they measure completely different things. 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D is the active hormone form — it circulates, does its job, gets cleared. 25-hydroxyvitamin D is your storage form, and it's the one that tells you whether you actually have enough reserves. Most doctors order the active form. It can look perfectly normal while your storage tanks are empty.

My 25-hydroxy was 28 ng/mL. The lab flagged it as "low" — their reference range starts at 30. My doctor mentioned it in passing, suggested I get more sun, and moved on to the next patient. I live in a place where the sun disappears for six months a year.

What actually worked: 5,000 IU daily of D3, taken with K2 (to direct calcium into bones instead of arteries) and magnesium (which is required for Vitamin D metabolism at multiple steps). Four months to go from 28 to 68 ng/mL. The effects were not subtle — mood improved, winter illnesses dropped to basically zero, and my free testosterone ticked up slightly, which makes sense given that Vitamin D is a steroid hormone precursor.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vitamin D (25-OH) | 30-100 ng/mL | 50-80 ng/mL | 28 | 68 |

## 5. HbA1c

Fasting glucose tells you about the last eight hours. HbA1c tells you about the last three months. It measures how much glucose has been stuck to your hemoglobin — a running average of your blood sugar control that can't be faked by fasting for 12 hours before your blood draw.

My fasting glucose was always fine. My HbA1c was 5.5%. The lab says anything under 5.7 is normal. That's true in the sense that 5.7 is the diabetes threshold. It's not true in the sense that every tenth of a point above 5.0% is associated with measurably higher all-cause mortality.[^5] At 5.5%, you're not diabetic, but you're also not in a metabolically healthy place.

[^5]: This relationship shows up consistently in large epidemiological studies. The Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration paper from 2010 (*The Lancet*) is probably the most cited, but there have been several replications since.

Getting from 5.5 to 5.0 required the same things as fixing my insulin (below): cutting refined carbohydrates, more protein earlier in the day, and — this one surprised me — fixing my sleep. Poor sleep raises cortisol, cortisol raises blood sugar, blood sugar raises HbA1c. Everything connects to everything in ways that make you want to scream.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HbA1c | <5.7% | <5.3% | 5.5% | 5.0% |

## 6. Fasting Insulin

This is the one I wish someone had tested when I was 25.

Fasting insulin catches insulin resistance years before your glucose starts rising. Here's what happens: your cells become less responsive to insulin, so your pancreas compensates by producing more of it. More insulin keeps your glucose stable, so your fasting glucose looks fine, but the insulin itself is doing damage — driving inflammation, promoting fat storage, raising SHBG (which tanks your free testosterone — see marker #3), and generally making everything worse. By the time your glucose finally climbs above the threshold, you've been insulin-resistant for a decade and you've accumulated a decade's worth of metabolic damage.

My fasting insulin was 12 µIU/mL. The lab range says anything under 25 is normal. Optimal is under 5. At 12, I was already significantly insulin-resistant, and based on my trajectory, I'd probably been heading this direction for 5-10 years.

I got from 12 to 3.2 over about a year. The interventions were boring: intermittent fasting (16:8), resistance training three times a week, and cutting seed oils, which is controversial but my inflammatory markers dropped faster after that change than after anything else I did. I don't know if seed oils are inherently bad or if cutting them just forced me to eat fewer processed foods. I do know my numbers improved.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fasting Insulin | <25 µIU/mL | <5 µIU/mL | 12.0 | 3.2 |

## 7. Full Thyroid Panel

TSH is a pituitary hormone, not a thyroid hormone. It tells you what your brain is asking your thyroid to do — not what your thyroid is actually doing in response.

A full panel requires five markers: TSH, free T3, free T4, reverse T3, and thyroid antibodies (TPO and/or thyroglobulin). T3 is the active hormone. T4 is the precursor. Reverse T3 is an inactive metabolite that binds to T3 receptors and blocks actual T3 from working. If your reverse T3 is elevated and your free T3 is low, you have a conversion problem — your body is turning T4 into the inactive form instead of the active one, even if your TSH and T4 look fine.

I've seen this pattern repeatedly in Research Collective members: TSH between 2 and 3 (completely unremarkable), free T3 at the very bottom of the range, reverse T3 above 15 ng/dL. These people are functionally hypothyroid with labs that look normal to any doctor who only checks TSH. The usual causes are stress (cortisol drives the T4→rT3 pathway), inflammation, and nutrient deficiencies (selenium and zinc are required for the T4→T3 conversion). Fix the underlying problem and the numbers normalize. Throw Synthroid at it without checking the full panel and you're treating the wrong thing.

| Marker | Standard Range | Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| TSH | 0.5-4.5 mIU/L | 0.5-2.0 |
| Free T3 | 2.0-4.4 pg/mL | 3.0-4.0 |
| Free T4 | 0.8-1.8 ng/dL | 1.0-1.5 |
| Reverse T3 | 10-24 ng/dL | <15 |
| TPO Antibodies | <35 IU/mL | <10 |

## 8. Ferritin

Ferritin is a weird marker because it does two contradictory things. It's your iron storage protein, so low ferritin means iron deficiency. But it's also an acute phase reactant — it rises during inflammation. So you can have "normal" ferritin that's actually low iron masked by inflammation, or "high" ferritin that isn't iron overload but just your body responding to some inflammatory process. You need to interpret it alongside HS-CRP to know which story you're looking at.

My ferritin was 38 ng/mL — technically within the reference range that starts at 15, but well below the optimal range of 70-100. At 38, I had restless legs every night, my sleep was garbage (verified by Oura ring — my deep sleep percentage was in the low teens), and I'd get winded going up stairs despite normal hemoglobin. I wasn't anemic. I was iron-deficient without anemia, which is a thing that exists and that most doctors dismiss because the hemoglobin is fine.

Iron bisglycinate, 25mg every other day, got me from 38 to 82 in three months. The every-other-day dosing is important — daily iron supplementation triggers hepcidin release, which blocks absorption for about 24 hours. Skipping a day actually increases total absorption. The restless legs stopped. My deep sleep went from 12% to 21%. Exercise stopped feeling like drowning.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ferritin | 15-150 ng/mL | 70-100 ng/mL | 38 | 82 |

## 9. Magnesium RBC

Serum magnesium is almost useless as a nutritional marker. Your body maintains blood magnesium within a tight range by pulling it from your bones and tissues, so your serum level can be perfectly normal while your cells are running on empty.

Magnesium RBC measures what's actually inside your red blood cells. It's a better proxy for tissue levels. Mine was 4.2 mg/dL while my serum magnesium was dead center of the reference range. Optimal RBC magnesium is above 6.0.

The difference between a serum test that says "fine" and an RBC test that says "depleted" is the difference between your biochemistry running at partial capacity across over 300 enzymatic reactions that use magnesium as a cofactor. That includes everything from energy production to neurotransmitter synthesis to DNA repair.

Magnesium glycinate at night (400mg) brought my RBC from 4.2 to 5.8 over four months. I added magnesium threonate in the morning for the cognitive effects — it crosses the blood-brain barrier better than other forms. Different forms do different things: glycinate is best for sleep and overall repletion, threonate for cognition, citrate for constipation, oxide for basically nothing except emptying your bowels. Don't buy oxide.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Serum Mg | 1.7-2.2 mg/dL | — | 1.9 | 2.1 |
| RBC Mg | 4.0-6.4 mg/dL | >6.0 | 4.2 | 5.8 |

## 10. Omega-3 Index

I eat fish. I thought this meant my omega-3 levels were fine.

My Omega-3 Index — the percentage of EPA and DHA in my red blood cell membranes — was 4.2%. The lab called this "adequate." For comparison, the Japanese population averages around 9-10% and has dramatically lower rates of cardiovascular disease and cognitive decline.[^6] The evidence suggests that going from 4% to 8% reduces major cardiovascular events by roughly 30%. That's not a small effect.

[^6]: This is the JELIS study and its follow-ups, which are worth reading in full. The short version: adding 1.8g/day of EPA to statin therapy reduced major coronary events by 19% in a Japanese population that already had high baseline omega-3 levels. The effect would almost certainly be larger in Western populations with lower baselines.

Most fish oil on shelves is already oxidized by the time you buy it. Rancid fish oil is worse than useless — the oxidation products are pro-inflammatory. I use a brand that publishes batch-level oxidation test results and I still keep it in the refrigerator. Three grams daily of combined EPA+DHA brought my index from 4.2% to 10.1% in five months.

I also started eating sardines, which I do not enjoy. The numbers don't care about my preferences.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Omega-3 Index | >4% | >8% | 4.2% | 10.1% |

## 11. ApoB

LDL cholesterol tells you how much cholesterol is inside your LDL particles. ApoB tells you how many LDL particles you actually have. If your LDL-C is normal but your ApoB is high, you have a lot of small, dense LDL particles — the ones that can squeeze into the arterial wall and initiate atherosclerosis. Big fluffy LDL particles are relatively harmless. Small dense ones are not.

About 25% of people have discordance between LDL-C and ApoB, meaning their LDL-C number gives them a false sense of security. You can't know which group you're in without measuring both.

My LDL-C was fine. My ApoB was 84 mg/dL — technically within the standard range (<90) but well above optimal (<60). I got from 84 to 52 by reducing saturated fat and increasing soluble fiber — oatmeal, psyllium husk, beans. No drugs. The lipid system is more responsive to diet than most people think. You just have to measure the right things to see it working.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ApoB | <90 mg/dL | <60 mg/dL | 84 | 52 |

## 12. Zinc

Zinc is involved in immune function, testosterone production, and dopamine signaling.[^7] Most of your body's zinc is intracellular, so serum zinc is an imperfect measure, but if serum zinc is low you definitely have a problem.

[^7]: The dopamine connection is underappreciated. Zinc is a cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme in dopamine synthesis. There's also evidence that zinc modulates dopamine transporter function and D2 receptor binding. If you're running any protocol that targets the dopamine system, you want your zinc status dialed in.

Mine was 68 µg/dL. The reference range starts at 60. Nobody flagged it. But optimal is 90-120, and at 68 I was almost certainly functionally deficient, especially since I was on an ACE inhibitor at the time (blood pressure medications increase urinary zinc excretion).

Supplementing zinc is tricky because too much depletes copper. I settled on 30mg of zinc picolinate, five days on and two days off, which got me from 68 to 98 in three months. I also stopped the ACE inhibitor once my blood pressure normalized from all the other interventions — fixing insulin resistance tends to fix blood pressure as a side effect, which is convenient.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zinc | 60-120 µg/dL | 90-120 µg/dL | 68 | 98 |

## The Pattern

Here's what $15,000 in bloodwork and 200+ member panels have taught me: the single most common profile I see is normal standard labs and terrible optimal labs. Someone who has been told they're fine. Someone whose inflammation is slowly climbing, whose insulin is slowly rising, whose free testosterone is slowly falling. Normal, normal, normal — until one day it isn't, and by then they've lost years of ground they can't easily get back.

I was that person. Most people walking around with "normal" bloodwork are.

The 12 markers above explain roughly 60% of the "I'm doing everything right and I still feel terrible" cases I see in new Research Collective members. Not because the other 40% doesn't matter. Because if you get these 12 right, the rest usually follows. They're the leverage points — the measurements that, when optimized, pull everything else in the right direction.

I'm not going to tell you this is easy. It took me three years and more money than I want to think about. But I am going to tell you it's possible, and that the alternative — flying blind with your own biology because nobody ever told you there was a better way — is a strange thing to accept when better tests exist and cost a few hundred dollars.
