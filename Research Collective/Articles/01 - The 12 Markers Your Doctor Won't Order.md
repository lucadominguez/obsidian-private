# What Your Bloodwork Is Actually Telling You (And What It Isn't)

**Published:** Week 1, Monday — Flagship article
**Type:** Free — Authority-building, shareable
**Lead Magnet:** "12 Lab Markers Checklist PDF"

---

I spent $15,000 on bloodwork before I understood what I was looking at.

Not an exaggeration. Three years. Monthly panels. 47 markers tracked. And for the first two of those years, I was reading the results completely wrong.

Here's the problem. When your doctor runs a "full panel" and tells you everything is normal, what they actually mean is: nothing is acutely killing you right now. The reference ranges are statistical averages of a population that includes diabetics, the metabolically damaged, and people walking around with systemic inflammation they don't know about. Being average in a sick population isn't the flex it sounds like.

I know because I was "normal" too. While gaining 40 pounds. While my cognition was sliding. While I was spiraling into a depression that six different antidepressants couldn't touch. Normal. Completely normal. Nothing to see here.

These are the 12 markers that actually moved — the ones where "normal" and "optimal" are different conversations entirely. I'm including my own numbers from before I knew what I was doing and after I figured things out. The difference between those two columns is the difference between feeling like garbage and feeling like myself again.

## 1. HS-CRP

Regular CRP tells you if you have an infection. The high-sensitivity version tells you if your body is quietly on fire.

My standard CRP was "normal." My HS-CRP was 3.2 mg/L. For reference, anything above 3.0 is considered high cardiovascular risk. For optimal — and I mean actually optimal, not just "not actively dying" — you want it under 0.5. I've since gotten mine to 0.4. Took about six months of systematic inflammation hunting.

The weird thing is you can feel fine with an HS-CRP of 3. You don't notice the inflammation until it's been there for years and suddenly your brain doesn't work the way it used to. By then you've lost ground you can't easily get back.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HS-CRP | <3.0 mg/L | <0.5 mg/L | 3.2 | 0.4 |

## 2. Homocysteine

This one still annoys me. I had mine tested because I read a paper linking elevated homocysteine to cognitive decline (it's a solid predictor), and it came back at 12.1 µmol/L. The lab flagged it as normal. It is not normal. Under 7 is what you want. Above 10 and you're looking at roughly double the risk of Alzheimer's compared to someone under 7.

What got mine down was methylated B vitamins — specifically methylfolate and methyl-B12. Regular B-complex didn't move it at all. Some people can't methylate properly (MTHFR mutations — yes that's the real acronym), and if you're one of them, standard B vitamins do almost nothing for homocysteine. You won't know unless you test.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Homocysteine | <15 µmol/L | <7 µmol/L | 12.1 | 6.8 |

## 3. Free Testosterone

Total T is a lie. Or at least, it's half the picture. My total was 520 ng/dL — smack in the middle of the 300-1000 reference range. Textbook normal. My free T — the stuff that actually gets into your cells instead of being bound up by SHBG — was 8.2 ng/dL. For reference, optimal free T is above 15.

So I had "normal" testosterone that wasn't actually reaching my tissues. I felt like garbage. Low energy. Bad recovery from exercise. Brain fog. Every standard metric said I was fine.

Lowering SHBG is its own project (boron helped, so did fixing my insulin — see #6), but the point is you can't evaluate testosterone from total T alone. Anyone who tells you different is operating on incomplete information.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total T | 300-1000 ng/dL | 500-900 | 520 | 580 |
| Free T | — | >15 ng/dL | 8.2 | 16.1 |

## 4. Vitamin D (25-Hydroxy)

Most doctors order 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D. That's the active form. It can look fine while your stored levels — 25-hydroxy — are in the basement. You need both, or at minimum the storage form.

My 25-hydroxy was 28 ng/mL when I first checked. The lab range says 30-100 is normal, so I was technically flagged "low." My doctor said I was "a little low" and told me to get more sun. I live in the Pacific Northwest. There is no sun.

When I got serious about supplementation — 5,000 IU daily with K2 and magnesium — it took about four months to get from 28 to 68. The difference was not subtle. Better mood. Fewer winter colds. My testosterone even ticked up slightly (Vitamin D is a hormone precursor — it feeds into the whole steroidogenesis pathway).

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vitamin D (25-OH) | 30-100 ng/mL | 50-80 ng/mL | 28 | 68 |

## 5. HbA1c

Fasting glucose is what they check at your annual physical. It tells you about the last 8 hours. HbA1c tells you about the last three months — it's a running average of how much sugar has been glommed onto your red blood cells.

My fasting glucose was always fine. My HbA1c was 5.5%. Fine by lab standards (under 5.7 is "normal"). But every tenth of a point above 5.0% correlates with increased all-cause mortality. At 5.5%, you're not diabetic. You're also not in a good place.

This one came down with the same interventions that fixed my insulin (see below): cutting refined carbs, more protein at breakfast, and — this surprised me — better sleep. Poor sleep raises cortisol, cortisol raises blood sugar, blood sugar raises HbA1c. Everything is connected in ways that make you want to pull your hair out.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HbA1c | <5.7% | <5.3% | 5.5% | 5.0% |

## 6. Fasting Insulin

This is the one I wish I'd tested ten years ago. Fasting insulin catches insulin resistance years before your glucose starts climbing. Your pancreas just works harder. You feel fine. But behind the scenes, you're pumping out more and more insulin to keep glucose stable, and that insulin itself is driving inflammation, weight gain, and hormonal dysfunction.

My fasting insulin was 12 µIU/mL — lab says under 25 is normal. Optimal is under 5. At 12, I was already insulin resistant and had been for probably a decade. This marker alone explained my weight gain, my SHBG elevation (insulin drives SHBG up, which tanks free T), and my inflammatory markers.

Getting from 12 to 3.2 took about a year. Intermittent fasting helped. So did resistance training. So did cutting seed oils — and I know that one's controversial, but my inflammatory markers dropped faster after that change than after anything else I tried.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fasting Insulin | <25 µIU/mL | <5 µIU/mL | 12.0 | 3.2 |

## 7. Full Thyroid Panel

TSH alone is garbage. I don't know how else to say it. It's a pituitary hormone, not a thyroid hormone. It tells you what your brain is asking for, not what your thyroid is delivering.

You need TSH, free T3, free T4, reverse T3, and thyroid antibodies — all five. I've seen multiple members in the Research Collective with TSH between 2 and 3 (normal!) whose free T3 was barely above the bottom of the range and whose reverse T3 was elevated. Reverse T3 is inactive — it binds to T3 receptors and blocks actual T3 from doing its job. So you have "normal" labs but functionally low thyroid.

If your reverse T3 is above 15 ng/dL and your free T3 is below 3.0 pg/mL, you have a conversion problem. Your body is turning T4 into reverse T3 instead of active T3. Stress, inflammation, and nutrient deficiencies all drive this. Fix the underlying problem and the numbers normalize. Throw Synthroid at it and you're treating the wrong thing.

| Marker | Standard Range | Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| TSH | 0.5-4.5 mIU/L | 0.5-2.0 |
| Free T3 | 2.0-4.4 pg/mL | 3.0-4.0 |
| Free T4 | 0.8-1.8 ng/dL | 1.0-1.5 |
| Reverse T3 | 10-24 ng/dL | <15 |
| TPO Antibodies | <35 IU/mL | <10 |

## 8. Ferritin

Ferritin is a weird one. It's your iron storage protein, but it also spikes during inflammation (it's an acute phase reactant). So low ferritin means you're iron deficient. High ferritin could mean iron overload, or it could mean inflammation. You need to look at it alongside HS-CRP to know which.

My ferritin was 38 ng/mL when I first tested. The standard range starts at 15, so I was told it was fine. It was not fine. At 38, I had restless legs at night, my sleep quality was garbage, and I'd get winded walking up stairs. I wasn't anemic — my hemoglobin was normal — but I was iron deficient.

I got it from 38 to 82 through supplementation (iron bisglycinate, every other day — daily dosing actually reduces absorption through hepcidin feedback). Took about three months. The restless legs stopped. My exercise tolerance improved noticeably. My Oura ring sleep scores went from low 70s to mid 80s.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ferritin | 15-150 ng/mL | 70-100 ng/mL | 38 | 82 |

## 9. Magnesium RBC

Serum magnesium is a lie. Your body will strip magnesium from your bones and tissues to keep blood levels stable, so your serum test can be perfect while you're severely depleted at the cellular level. Magnesium RBC measures what's actually inside your red blood cells — it's a better proxy for tissue levels.

My serum magnesium was normal. My RBC magnesium was 4.2 mg/dL (optimal is above 6.0). That means despite normal blood levels, my cells were running on partial power for over 300 enzymatic reactions that depend on magnesium as a cofactor.

Magnesium glycinate at night (400mg) got my RBC from 4.2 to 5.8 over about four months. Magnesium threonate in the morning helped with the cognitive side. Different forms do different things. Glycinate is best for sleep and overall repletion. Threonate crosses the blood-brain barrier better. Citrate will give you diarrhea. Oxide is basically a laxative and barely absorbable.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Serum Mg | 1.7-2.2 mg/dL | — | 1.9 | 2.1 |
| RBC Mg | 4.0-6.4 mg/dL | >6.0 | 4.2 | 5.8 |

## 10. Omega-3 Index

This one surprised me. I eat fish. I thought I was fine. My Omega-3 Index — the percentage of EPA and DHA in my red blood cell membranes — was 4.2%. The lab flagged it as "adequate." Optimal is above 8%.

The gap between 4% and 8% is approximately a 30% reduction in major cardiovascular events. That's not a rounding error. That's the difference between "you're probably fine" and "you've actually protected yourself."

High-quality fish oil (I use a brand that publishes third-party oxidation tests — most fish oil on shelves is already rancid) got me from 4.2% to 10.1% in about five months. 3 grams daily of EPA+DHA combined. I also started eating sardines, which I don't enjoy, but the numbers don't care about my preferences.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Omega-3 Index | >4% | >8% | 4.2% | 10.1% |

## 11. ApoB

LDL cholesterol tells you how much cholesterol is in your LDL particles. ApoB tells you how many LDL particles you have. If you have normal LDL-C but high ApoB, you have a lot of small, dense LDL particles. Those are the ones that squeeze into your arterial wall and cause atherosclerosis. Big fluffy LDL particles are relatively harmless. Small dense ones are not.

My LDL-C was normal. My ApoB was 84 mg/dL — technically within the standard <90 reference range, but well above the optimal <60. About 25% of people have this discordance between LDL-C and ApoB. You can't know unless you measure both.

I got ApoB from 84 to 52 by cutting saturated fat and increasing fiber. Nothing exotic. No drugs. Just diet. The body's lipid system is more responsive to dietary changes than most people realize — you just have to actually measure the right things to see it working.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ApoB | <90 mg/dL | <60 mg/dL | 84 | 52 |

## 12. Zinc

Zinc is involved in immune function, testosterone production, and dopamine signaling. Most of your zinc is inside cells, so serum zinc is an imperfect measure — but if serum zinc is low, you know there's a problem.

Mine was 68 µg/dL. Lab range starts at 60. Nobody flagged it. But optimal is 90-120. At 68, I was probably functionally deficient, especially since I was on an ACE inhibitor at the time (blood pressure medication that increases zinc excretion).

Zinc supplementation is tricky. Too much and you deplete copper. Too little and nothing changes. I landed on 30mg of zinc picolinate daily, cycled (5 days on, 2 off), which got me from 68 to 98 over about three months. I also stopped the ACE inhibitor once my blood pressure normalized from the other interventions.

| | Standard Range | Optimal | My First | My Current |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zinc | 60-120 µg/dL | 90-120 µg/dL | 68 | 98 |

## The Pattern Nobody Talks About

Here's what $15,000 in bloodwork taught me: the single most common profile I see — in myself, in the 200+ panels I've reviewed from Research Collective members — is normal standard labs and terrible optimal labs. Someone who "feels fine" according to their doctor. Someone whose inflammation is slowly climbing. Whose insulin is slowly rising. Whose free testosterone is slowly falling. Normal, normal, normal — until one day it isn't, and by then they've lost a decade of ground they can't easily recover.

I was that person.

The 12 markers above explain roughly 60% of the "I'm doing everything right and I still feel terrible" posts I see from new members. Not because the other 40% doesn't matter — because if you get these 12 right, everything else usually follows.

## If You're Going to Test Anything

You don't need a doctor to order these. Discount labs exist specifically because the insurance-based system won't cover optimization. Marek Health, Own Your Labs, Ulta Lab Tests — any of them will run this panel for maybe $200 to $400 total. Your insurance-funded annual physical covers three of these at best, and it'll report them against ranges that include sick people.

Get baselines before you start any protocol. If you don't know where you started, you can't claim anything changed. One abnormal result is a data point. Three in a row is a pattern. Track trends.

I test monthly because I run compounds that can shift these markers fast. You might not need monthly. But you need something. Flying blind with your own biology is a strange thing to accept when the alternative costs less than a nice dinner.
