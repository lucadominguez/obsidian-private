## 1. Martin's Newsletter (Martin Shkreli)
**URL:** martinshkreli.substack.com
**Articles analyzed:** "SF Trip: March 2026 - Part 1: Agents" (Mar 16, 2026), "SF Trip: March 2026 - Part 2: Peptides" (Mar 17, 2026)
**Status:** Active, free

### Opening Style
Shkreli opens with provocative, voice-first statements that read like someone grabbing you by the collar at a bar. "Have you ever had the thing that you know a lot about become *the current thing*?" or "San Francisco's tech scene is your favorite manic depressive artist: crazy but speaks an indelible truth." He announces his authority immediately ("I've spent the last few months studying...") and often throws in a casual incredulity marker — "Holy shit, I don't know where to start" — that functions like a verbal handshake. No preamble, no throat-clearing. He skips the formal introduction and speaks as if resuming an interrupted conversation with a friend.

### Sentence Rhythm
Highly varied, deliberately erratic. Short declarative sentences ("Drug companies like to make money.") collide with long, looping analytical passages that pile clauses. He uses sentence fragments as punctuation ("Really?" "No. Stop it.") and italicized emphasis constantly, creating a herky-jerky cadence that mirrors real speech. The rhythm is argumentative: assertion, then qualification, then restatement with more force. Paragraphs are short and designed for skimming — he rarely goes more than 4-5 sentences without a break. The overall feel is of someone thinking out loud but editing for punch.

### Data Handling
Domain expertise IS his data. Rather than cite sources, Shkreli asserts from personal knowledge: "I spent twenty years looking at pharmaceuticals." When he does use numbers — "Peptides half extremely short half-lives, often on the order of seconds or minutes" — they come from his own mental database. He deploys technical terminology (pharmacokinetics, therapeutic hypothesis, assays, target engagement, double-blind placebo controlled) as authority markers, assuming readers will either keep up or trust him. He sometimes makes errors in transcription ("Peptides half extremely short half-lives") that read like uncorrected dictation, adding to the raw, unpolished feel.

### What Makes the Voice Unmistakable
Shkreli writes exactly like he talks on video: combative, erudite, petty, and disconcertingly charming. The voice is defined by *performative contempt* — for peptide enthusiasts, for journalists, for the uneducated — coupled with genuine deep knowledge that earns him the right to be contemptuous. He alternates between "I am the expert, listen to me" and "but I'm just a regular guy, look how casual I am." The tonal whiplash IS the brand. He'll spend 500 words on molecular binding mechanics, then pivot to "But wahhh." The voice says: I'm smarter than you, I know it, but I'm also funny about it, so you'll stay.

### Verbal Tics
- "Holy shit" as transition/intensifier
- Mock-quoting opponents: "But Martin, I want to optimize my health!"
- "No. Stop it." as paragraph closer
- "Look, I'm not a softy" before delivering hard truths
- Parenthetical asides that read like whispered confessions
- Deliberate typos/transcription errors that signal authenticity
- The word "delusion" deployed as diagnosis, not insult
- Bold formatting for key concepts (pharmacokinetics, therapeutic hypothesis)
- "I have zero doubt" / "There is no doubt" as rhetorical hammer
- Closing each section with a declarative slam: "This shit has to end."

### Personal/Research Balance
Heavily weighted to personal authority over external research. Shkreli rarely cites others except to mock them. His research IS his biography — "I've spent the last few months studying photonic computing" or "I spent twenty years looking at pharmaceuticals." When he cites external facts (Siga stock, monkeypox data, portfolio returns), they're his own observations. In his older article about being hacked, he veers into deeply personal grievance territory, naming specific journalists (Matt Levine, Molly White) and detailing his legal response. The ratio is roughly 80% personal stance / 20% external reference.

### Transition Style
Section breaks with horizontal rules and H2 subheadings ("Pharma Basics", "Screw the FDA and pharma. *Really?*"). Within sections, transitions are conversational rather than logical — he'll end one thought with a punchline and start the next with a new provocation. Often uses the reader's presumed objection as the transition: "But Martin, there has to be some unapproved drug out there..." The technique is Socratic interrogation of a straw man.

### Signature Move
**The straw-man dialogue.** Shkreli invents his opponents' arguments, voices them sarcastically, and then demolishes them. "'But wahhh.' *There are actual dying people in the world.*" This creates the illusion of debate while ensuring he always wins. Coupled with genuine expertise, it makes for compelling reading even when you disagree.

## 3. Dominic Cummings
**URL:** dominiccummings.substack.com
**Articles analyzed:** "People, Ideas, Machines XVI: ideas from Lee Kuan Yew on how to rescue Britain" (May 31, 2026), "People, Ideas, Machines VII: The Wizard War - RV Jones and the Most Secret War" (partial)
**Status:** Active, free

### Opening Style
Cummings opens with a pull-quote epigraph — typically from the subject of the essay, a historical figure, or a secondary authority like Charlie Munger. This functions as both thesis statement and intellectual credential. Then he launches into a dense, historically-situated opening paragraph that connects the reader's present concerns (Brexit, British decline, Whitehall dysfunction) to deeper historical patterns. "A decade after the referendum, I re-read notes from LKY's memoirs..." His openings are long, multi-sentence constructions that assume high reader commitment. No hooks, no teasers — he begins as if writing for people who have already decided to read.

### Sentence Rhythm
Long, clause-heavy, almost Germanic in its nesting. Cummings writes sentences that routinely exceed 40-50 words, with parenthetical asides, em-dash interruptions, and semicolon chains. The rhythm is academic-argumentative — he builds layered claims, qualifies them, then restates. He uses numbered lists extensively (1 through 13+ points) and bold inline headings to impose structure on what would otherwise be stream-of-consciousness polemic. Paragraphs are long (10+ sentences) and dense. Reading Cummings requires sustained focus; this is not writing optimized for phones.

### Data Handling
Cummings treats *history as data*. His evidence is overwhelmingly historical — LKY's memoirs, RV Jones's accounts of WWII scientific intelligence, Alanbrooke's diaries, General Groves's Manhattan Project memoir. He quotes extensively and precisely, often reproducing entire paragraphs from source material. He also draws on personal experience as data ("I found the same thing in the Department for Education 2010-14"). Statistics appear rarely and are usually rough estimates ("10X to 100X faster"). The data handling says: I have read more than you, I remember it better than you, and the patterns are clear if you do the reading.

### What Makes the Voice Unmistakable
Cummings writes like a systems theorist who accidentally acquired power and is now furious about losing it. The voice is defined by: (1) intellectual arrogance that is earned through extraordinary breadth of reading, (2) granular insider knowledge of how UK government actually works (office names, specific meetings, WhatsApp logs), (3) a persistent framing of all problems as *regime* problems not *policy* problems, and (4) undisguised contempt for "SW1" (Westminster), "NPCs," the "mainstream," and essentially everyone who is not a small cadre of elite operators. He uses italics for emphasis more than any other writer analyzed — nearly every sentence has italicized stress. The cumulative impression is of a brilliant, aggrieved autodidact who cannot stop explaining why everyone else is wrong.

### Verbal Tics
- "Inter alia" (recurring Latinism)
- Em-dash interruptions that pile qualifications within sentences
- "NB." as a discursive side-note marker
- "The X crowd that told you Y then Z — this crowd also believes..."
- Sweeping categories: "SW1," "the mainstream," "the Remainer-SW1-NPC world," "the right"
- Italicized emphasis on nearly every key term
- Bold sub-headings mid-paragraph: "**All wrong.**"
- References to "trolley" (Liz Truss), "Trolley-Carrie" and other nicknaming of political figures
- Historical analogies deployed as argument ("Bismarck-level performance")
- The construction "X is/was/were and Y is/was/were" for parallel contrasts

### Personal/Research Balance
Approximately 60% historical research / 40% personal testimony. Cummings frequently moves between quoting LKY or RV Jones and inserting his own No10 experience. This is the distinctive move: "Here is what the great minds knew — and here is what I tried to implement before the idiots stopped me." The personal interjections are always framed as case studies that prove the historical pattern. He is unusually transparent about process: "I've bodged this together and edited as I've gone. It's now 99% final."

### Transition Style
Numbered lessons with bold headings. Section breaks use horizontal rules. Within sections, transitions are often abrupt — he'll exhaust one topic and pivot with "Also to ponder:" or "There's another general issue." The numbered structure (1-13) provides coherence amid rambling. He frequently uses "E.g." to launch into illustrative examples that run for paragraphs. The overall architecture is that of an annotated reading list, not a polished essay.

### Signature Move
**The Meta-Lesson of Performance.** Cummings's unique rhetorical pattern is: (1) extract a specific lesson from history, (2) show how he applied it in government, (3) document how the bureaucracy destroyed it after he left, (4) argue that this destruction is not an accident but an iron law of large organizations. This creates a tragic-heroic narrative where Cummings is the Cassandra who was right, was briefly in power, and was then undone by the forces he diagnosed. His post "The Great Meta-Lesson of Performance and Government" is the purest expression — the argument that every successful wartime innovation (RV Jones's unit, Groves's Manhattan Project structures, NASA's Apollo culture) was immediately dismantled after victory by the bureaucracy, and that the same pattern recurred with COVID.

## 4. Chamath Palihapitiya
**URL:** chamath.substack.com
**Articles analyzed:** "Deep Dive: A Primer On The Agentic AI Economy" (May 12, 2026), "Deep Dive: Where Value Accrues in the AI Stack" (May 1, 2026)
**Status:** Active, mostly paid ($99/month)

### Opening Style
Chamath opens with a concrete, surprising fact: "On a Friday evening in November 2025, Peter Steinberger built the first version of OpenClaw. The prototype only took about an hour..." He leads with velocity — the speed of change is his hook. This is classic venture-capital framing: identify the moment the world changed, anchor it to a specific person/company, then zoom out to the structural implications. His second article opens with historical analogy: "Well, the first generation of internet companies had a map. It was called the OSI stack..." The pattern is: specific example -> analogy -> structural framework -> investment thesis.

### Sentence Rhythm
Clean, declarative, and consciously authoritative. Short paragraphs (3-4 sentences max). He uses line breaks aggressively — nearly every sentence could stand alone. The rhythm is that of a pitch deck translated into prose: fact, implication, framework, call to action. No digressions, no parenthetical asides, no conversational tangents. Every sentence advances the argument. This is the most "edited" voice in the batch — it reads as if it has been through multiple revisions for clarity and impact.

### Data Handling
Data-forward with precise numbers. "Anthropic went from $1B to $44B in annualized revenue in seventeen months." "The price of running a model has dropped 1,500x in six years." "McKinsey's 2025 State of AI survey found that fewer than 10% of organizations have agents deployed at meaningful scale." He cites specific companies, specific metrics, and specific dates. But the data always serves the framework — it is illustrative, not exploratory. He's not wondering what the data means; he's using data to prove a thesis he already holds. The "Deep Dive PDF" that follows each post (84 pages, 138 pages) signals that the research exists and is massive, but the public-facing writing is the executive summary.

### What Makes the Voice Unmistakable
Chamath writes like someone who needs to be right at scale. The voice is characterized by: (1) total certainty about structural trends, (2) layered frameworks ("five-layer framework," "six layers, from the bottom up"), (3) historical analogies that position the present as a repeat of past wealth-creation moments ("Rockefeller had 90% of refining by 1880, Cisco had 85% of routing by 2000"), and (4) an implicit promise that reading his analysis will help you position capital before the crowd. There's no humor, no self-deprecation, no personal disclosure. The voice is *institutional* rather than personal — it could be a white paper from Social Capital. What distinguishes it from a research report is the first-person "we" and the framing of analysis as competitive advantage.

### Verbal Tics
- "The question is..." / "The opportunity is..." (framing moves)
- "Here is what you will find inside:" (table of contents as prose)
- Named frameworks: "the five-layer framework," "the six layers"
- "fulcrum assets" as recurring concept
- "Read below and let me know what you think in the group."
- "Chamath" as signature (first name only, conversational)
- Mandatory disclaimer block at the end of every post
- Historical name-drops as authority signals: "Intel. Cisco. Broadcom. Oracle."

### Personal/Research Balance
Approximately 10% personal framing / 90% research presentation. Chamath's personal experience appears only as implied authority ("we spent the first quarter of 2026 answering these questions") or in brief references to his company ("including my company, 8090"). The writing is deliberately depersonalized — the insights are presented as objective analysis from a team, not as an individual's opinions. This contrasts sharply with every other writer in this batch.

### Transition Style
Clean and syllabus-like. He uses questions to pivot: "So what is an AI agent, exactly?" "Where does it start? Where does it end? Where does it fork?" The transitions are explicit and instructional — he tells you what the next section will cover before delivering it. This is the writing of someone who thinks in frameworks and wants to transfer a mental model intact.

### Signature Move
**The Stack Framework.** Chamath's signature intellectual move is drawing boundaries around a chaotic frontier and naming the layers. He doesn't predict specific winners — he identifies *where winners will be determined*. The "six-layer stack" (infrastructure, chips, data, models, execution, application) and the "five-layer framework" for agents are his version of this. The reader walks away feeling oriented — the unknown has been partitioned into knowable competitions. This framing transforms anxiety about technological change into a game board with rules.

## 5. Predictive History Substack
**URL:** predictivehistory.substack.com
**Articles analyzed:** "Peter Thiel's Republic" (May 30, 2026), "The Loneliness of Mad King Trump" (May 23, 2026), "China's Third Center Strategy" (May 16, 2026), "The Acceleration" (Mar 7, 2026), "World War III Begins" (Feb 28, 2026), "Countdown to Iran" (Feb 21, 2026), "Welcome to the Rupture" (Feb 14, 2026), "Twilight of the Liberal Elite" (Feb 7, 2026), "The Great Unraveling" (Jan 31, 2026)
**Status:** Active, paid

### Opening Style
Predictive History opens with a news hook, a geopolitical event, or a biographical anecdote that serves as a case study of deeper forces. "This week, the New York Times reported that Peter Thiel has moved to Buenos Aires." "At around eight in the morning on Saturday, the Americans and Israelis bombarded Iran with missiles..." "By the time you read this, it is possible that Trump has attacked Cuba or Iran or both." The opening paragraph places the reader in a specific news moment, then immediately signals that conventional explanations are surface-level: "We are living in Peter Thiel's world. We just don't know it, and we never will." The second or third paragraph typically pivots to grand historical or occult framing.

### Sentence Rhythm
Measured, journalistic pacing with periodic acceleration into dramatic declamation. Paragraphs are short (4-6 sentences). Individual sentences are clean and well-constructed — subject, verb, object, with crisp dependent clauses. This makes the content (which is often extreme) feel sober and analytical. The technique is to write about apocalyptic scenarios in the tone of a foreign-policy briefing, creating a tension between form and content. Bolded sentences appear as thesis crystallizations: "Trump does not have a plan to win the war. He has a plan to lose this war to save the world."

### Data Handling
Hyperlink-heavy. Nearly every factual claim has an inline hyperlink to a news source (BBC, Reuters, NYT, WaPo, Times of Israel, Polymarket). This creates a dense, footnoted texture that signals rigor. But the data is always subordinated to the interpretive framework. Polymarket odds appear alongside news reports as equally valid data points. The treatment of information is that of a conspiracy researcher who has done the reading and wants you to know it — every claim is sourced, but the sources are selected to support a pre-existing narrative architecture. Primary sources mingle with fringe sources without distinction.

### What Makes the Voice Unmistakable
Predictive History writes from a position of *esoteric knowledge*. The voice is defined by: (1) the assumption that visible events are manifestations of hidden plans, (2) a specific vocabulary drawn from occult traditions ("New World Order," "the rupture," "Freemasons," "Satanic pedophilia," "Anglo-American empire"), (3) a geopolitical framework centered on great-power competition where the US is an empire in managed decline, (4) hyper-literacy in current events combined with a willingness to connect dots that mainstream analysts won't, and (5) a tone that is simultaneously alarmist and detached. The author positions themselves as someone who has seen the script. The byline is "Predictive History" — no individual name — which reinforces the institutional/oracular stance.

### Verbal Tics
- "Let me summarize the occult understanding of the world..."
- "I interpret this to mean..."
- "We can surmise..." / "We can imagine..."
- "It is safe to assume..."
- "This is our timeline, the timeline in which..."
- Book and film references as explanatory frameworks: "Think the book Brave New World meets the movie Her"
- Inline hyperlinks to news sources on nearly every claim
- Bold thesis statements mid-paragraph
- Apocalyptic periodization: "The Rupture," "The Acceleration," "The Great Unraveling"
- Parenthetical asides that insinuate more than they state: "(who has never had an original thought)"

### Personal/Research Balance
Approximately 15% personal / 85% research. The "I" appears sparingly — usually in the context of previous predictions: "Two years ago, I predicted a disastrous US ground invasion of Iran." The author rarely shares personal experience. The research is omnivorous: mainstream journalism, fringe blogs, Polymarket, historical texts, occult literature. The preceding lecture series on game theory is mentioned but not integrated. One revealing personal moment: "I need to apologize. In my last post, I referenced an e-mail... It turns out that the e-mail was a hoax." This admission of error is rare and notable.

### Transition Style
Paragraph breaks function as logical leaps rather than smooth transitions. Each paragraph introduces a new piece of evidence or a new layer of interpretation. The cumulative effect is of a dossier being assembled, not an argument being built. Abrupt shifts from news facts to occult interpretation are the norm: one paragraph describes US naval deployments, the next explains that "Freemasons (who control America's national security apparatus) revere the number '33'."

### Signature Move
**The Hidden Architecture Reveal.** Predictive History's signature technique is the pivot from surface-level news to deep-structure explanation. A post begins like conventional journalism (troop movements, diplomatic negotiations, stock prices), then suddenly the lens shifts: "Let me summarize the occult understanding of the world." The reader discovers that the visible story is a cover for the real one. This is conspiracy thinking elevated to literary form. The effectiveness comes from the contrast between meticulous sourcing of mundane facts and the audacity of the interpretive framework applied to them.

## 7. Investing in Compound Growth
**URL:** investingincompoundgrowth.substack.com
**Articles analyzed:** "The Ultimate Silver Heist: Why Honey Badger Silver is a Table-Pounding Buy Right Now" (May 18, 2026), "Finding the 10 bagger opportunity in the Canadian Mining Sector" (Jan 27, 2026)
**Status:** Active, free

### Opening Style
Opens with a direct thesis statement that combines authority with urgency. "If you want to make outsized returns in the junior mining sector, you only need to look for two things: catastrophic mispricing and the quiet accumulation of smart money." The second article opens with personal narrative: "I started investing back in 2010. I still remember some of the very first stocks I ever bought..." Both approaches establish credibility quickly — the first through framework, the second through autobiographical proof of learning. The tone is enthusiastic boosterism tempered by hard-won experience.

### Sentence Rhythm
Short, punchy, exclamation-point-friendly. This writer uses bold formatting aggressively — key numbers, stock tickers, and valuation metrics are bolded for scanning. Paragraphs are short (3-5 sentences) and designed for rapid consumption. The rhythm alternates between: (1) the hard sell ("Here is exactly why this stock is a table-pounding buy today"), (2) the data dump (PEA numbers, EBITDA calculations), and (3) the personal testimonial ("I also lived through multiple 50% drawdowns"). The cumulative effect is of a knowledgeable retail investor who has graduated from gambling to analysis and wants to bring you along.

### Data Handling
Heavily quantitative for a retail-oriented newsletter. The silver article includes: resource estimates (400M AgEq oz), acquisition price ($12M), per-ounce cost ($0.03/oz), annual production forecasts, EBITDA projections ($300-450MM+ USD/year), PEA assumptions, sensitivity tables. Charts and screenshots from corporate presentations are embedded. The data is presented as evidence of mispricing: "the market is wrong, and here are the numbers that prove it." The second article includes personal portfolio performance data shown via brokerage screenshots, lending authenticity. The data handling is sophisticated for the format but occasionally imprecise — numbers are rounded, sources are corporate presentations rather than independent verification.

### What Makes the Voice Unmistakable
The voice is that of a retail investor who found religion in mining stocks. It's defined by: (1) infectious excitement about specific trades, (2) the framing of every investment as a discovery ("highway robbery," "deal of the century," "most asymmetrical resource acquisition of the decade"), (3) a blend of sophisticated financial modeling and colloquial enthusiasm, (4) references to "smart money" (Eric Sprott) as validation, (5) genuine transparency about past failures, and (6) a Canadian perspective on resources (flow-through shares, TSX-V tickers). The writing feels like a highly competent Reddit DD post that graduated to a newsletter.

### Verbal Tics
- "Table-pounding buy" / "table-pounding buy today" (signature phrase)
- "Let's put that into perspective:" before data dumps
- Stock tickers in bold with exchange suffixes: **$TUF.v**, **$STND.v**
- "Here is exactly why..."
- Exclamation marks on valuation metrics
- "The Deal of the Century" / "highway robbery" (hyperbolic transaction framing)
- Screenshot embeds from brokerage accounts and corporate decks
- "I still believe..." / "I expect..." (forward-looking conviction markers)
- "Stay tuned — and don't forget to hit subscribe"

### Personal/Research Balance
Approximately 40% personal narrative / 60% financial analysis. The personal story is a key part of the pitch — the writer shares their journey from blowing up their account ($200k to -$20k shorting UVXY calls) to compounding at ~50% annually. This transparency creates trust. The research consists of reading corporate presentations, PEAs, financial statements, and tracking insider buying (Eric Sprott's purchases). There is no original investigative work — this is synthesis and repackaging of public information with a retail-investor lens.

### Transition Style
Simple, numbered, and hierarchical. Section headers (H3, H4) with clear topics: "1. The Deal of the Century," "Performance and Returns," "Summary." Horizontal rules separate major sections. Within sections, bulleted lists and bold inline headers provide structure. The transitions are utilitarian — this is writing organized for information delivery, not literary effect.

### Signature Move
**The Asymmetric Mispricing Argument.** The writer's signature analytical move is to identify a resource company trading at a fraction of its asset value using a specific metric (dollars per ounce in the ground, enterprise value relative to projected EBITDA). Then they layer on "smart money" validation (Sprott buying 10%), management quality signals, and macro tailwinds to build a multi-factor bull case. The posts read like investment committee memos translated for retail.

## 8. Rogue Funds (Jacob Rowe)
**URL:** roguefunds.substack.com
**Articles analyzed:** "Q1 2025 Letter to Investors" (Jun 13, 2025), "Rogue Funds Interview with Capital Employed" (Jun 19, 2024)
**Status:** Active, free (marketing for hedge fund)

### Opening Style
Formal but personal — these are investor letters republished as blog posts. Opens with a procedural note ("Sorry for the delay in releasing this publicly, last month has been a bit of a whirlwind with the honeymoon and wedding") then moves into the standard hedge-fund letter format: performance summary, fee structure disclosure, portfolio commentary. The interview opens with biographical narrative about learning to invest in high school. The tone is earnest, transparent, and slightly defensive in the way of young fund managers who know their age and lack of institutional pedigree are risk factors.

### Sentence Rhythm
Business-formal with periodic lapses into conversational directness. The quarterly letter follows the cadence of professional investor communications: measured, qualified, forward-looking statements hedged with caveats. But the interview is looser — Rowe speaks (writes) like a smart autodidact explaining his process. Sentences tend to be long and explanatory, with multiple clauses. He uses "I believe" and "I expect" frequently as conviction markers. The rhythm is steady and unflashy — this is writing designed to convey competence and care, not excitement.

### Data Handling
Performance data is central and detailed. Rowe reports: net returns, seed-fee-structure returns, quarterly volatility, concentration levels, position-by-position commentary with specific catalysts and timelines. He names specific companies (ASP Isotopes, Achieve Life Sciences, Green Data Centers, HAYPP Group, Aware Inc), provides market caps, revenue projections, catalyst dates, and risk assessments. The data is specific but unaudited — every post carries a disclaimer. He includes screenshots of performance charts. The data handling says: I am transparent, I track everything, and I want you to understand exactly what happened.

### What Makes the Voice Unmistakable
Rowe writes like a young value investor who studied Michael Burry's old blog posts and Joel Greenblatt's books. The voice is defined by: (1) extreme transparency about both wins and losses, (2) the framing of volatility as acceptable and expected ("I will continue to reiterate that I expect to see outsized volatility"), (3) a catalyst-driven value investing philosophy explained in plain language, (4) self-taught credentials worn openly ("I don't have any background in the financial industry, and I am mainly self-taught"), and (5) the simultaneous confidence of good returns (75% since inception net) and humility about age/experience. The voice says: I'm young, I'm learning, but my process works and the numbers prove it.

### Verbal Tics
- "I expect..." / "I believe..." (conviction markers used ~3-4x per page)
- "Going forward..." / "at this point" / "as I stated last quarter..."
- "I am extremely excited/excited about..."
- Thanking his fiancee for editing (personal touch in formal letter)
- Detailed fee structure recitation
- Mandatory disclaimer blocks
- "You can read more about this investment on the Rogue Funds blog here"
- Concentration and volatility as recurring themes (acknowledged but embraced)

### Personal/Research Balance
Approximately 30% personal/meta / 70% investment analysis. The personal content is largely confined to the interview and the letter's opening/closing. The investment analysis is detailed, position-by-position, with specific catalysts. The research style is that of a deep-value investor: read everything (10-Ks, transcripts, investor presentations, online write-ups), build a "story" of the company, look for 3-5x upside in a few years, throw out anything that doesn't meet the threshold.

### Transition Style
Standard hedge-fund letter conventions: performance summary, then sold-out positions, then core holdings breakdown, then new positions, then structural updates, then summary. Headings are used clearly (H3, H4). Internal transitions are direct: "As I stated last quarter," "Going forward," "Compared to last year at this time."

### Signature Move
**The Catalyst Timeline.** Rowe's distinctive analytical pattern is laying out a sequence of expected catalysts over multiple quarters, creating a narrative of stepwise value realization: "Silicon-28 will be sold in the coming weeks... Ytterbium at end of Q2 or beginning of Q3... TerraPower deal at any moment... Iceland within 3 months... QLE spinoff in late Q3 early Q4... NECSA MOU uranium pilot plant." This creates a feeling of inevitability — the thesis is not a single bet but a chain of events that compound.

## 9. Seabrick Dispatch
**URL:** seabrick.substack.com
**Articles analyzed:** "Welcome to The Seabrick Dispatch" (Jul 21, 2025)
**Status:** Active, free (only 1 post)

### Opening Style
Opens with a grand declarative paragraph that announces the publication's purpose: "Seasteading rose to ephemeral prominence in Silicon Valley in the 2010s, but has since been forgotten. Why? Economic and engineering hurdles are often blamed. But in truth it is a lack of gall that accounts for this pause in the exploration of the greatest frontier human civilization has yet known." The tone is manifesto-like — this is a founding document. The opening establishes the problem (climate change, bureaucratic inertia, stagnation) and positions Seabrick as the solution.

### Sentence Rhythm
The post is structured as a dialogue between a named interviewer (William Joy) and the founder (John Richardson). This Q&A format creates a natural, conversational rhythm. Richardson's answers are the substantive content — they are earnest, technically informed, and visionary in scope. His sentences are explanatory and patient: "Seabrick is an interlocking floating brick technology made of renewable materials. Its objective is to provide infrastructure for marine habitats, aquaculture operations, docks — all sorts of floating infrastructure." The rhythm is that of a founder pitch extended into long-form: explanation, vision, explanation, vision.

### Data Handling
Richardson provides specific numbers to ground the vision: 50% carbon by weight in Seabricks, 3.6 kg CO2 per kg of carbon, 1 million tons of flax fiber annually in Canada, 5 billion tons of global waste fiber, 2 feet/day kelp growth, 1 million square kilometers of kelp farming could sequester all annual emissions, 8% of expected agricultural land loss. The math is back-of-envelope but internally consistent and clearly communicated. The data is presented as proof-of-possibility, not as rigorous scientific validation.
