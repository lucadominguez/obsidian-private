I had $87,000 in debt.

On $87,000, that's $13,000 to $22,000 in fees — and most of what they do (send letters, negotiate on your behalf) you can do yourself. Some are outright scams. The legitimate ones are just expensive. I tried one. They settled one account in six months and charged me $2,000 for the privilege. I fired them and learned to do it myself.

This article is not legal advice. I'm not a lawyer. I'm someone who climbed out of a financial hole that felt impossible and documented every step. What follows is strategy, not legal counsel. Consult an attorney if you're being sued.

## How the debt industry actually works

Most people think of debt as a moral failure. They borrowed money, they couldn't pay it back, they're irresponsible. The debt industry loves this framing because it makes you feel ashamed, and ashamed people don't fight back.

Here's the truth: consumer debt is a product. It's bought and sold on a secondary market between banks, collection agencies, and debt buyers. When you default on a credit card, the original bank doesn't chase you for years. They charge off the account after 180 days, take a tax write-off for the loss, and sell your debt to a collection agency for pennies on the dollar — literally 4 to 7 cents on the dollar.[^2] The collection agency then tries to collect the full amount from you, because their profit margin depends on collecting more than they paid.

[^2]: This is the industry standard. A portfolio of charged-off credit card debt typically sells for 4-7% of face value. Medical debt sells for even less — 2-3%. The older the debt, the cheaper it is. Debts past the statute of limitations sell for less than 1%. The buyer's entire business model depends on collecting enough from enough people to cover the losses on the ones who don't pay. Your $10,000 debt cost them about $500.

This secondary market creates a paperwork problem for the collectors. Every time a debt is sold, the documentation has to transfer with it: the original contract, the complete payment history, proof that the current collector owns the debt. Debt gets sold and resold, sometimes three or four times, and the paperwork... degrades. Gets lost. Gets incomplete. This is not your problem. It's theirs. And it's your biggest weapon.

## The debt validation letter

Federal law — the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, specifically 15 USC 1692g — gives you the right to demand proof that a debt is yours. Here's how it works:

1. A debt collector contacts you — by mail or by phone.
2. Within 30 days, you send them a Debt Validation Letter. Certified mail. Return receipt requested.
3. They MUST stop all collection activity until they provide the proof.
4. Proof means: the original contract with your signature, a complete payment history from the original creditor, and documentation showing they legally own the debt.

The FDCPA is clear on this. If they can't validate, they can't collect. They can't report it to credit bureaus. They can't call you. They can't sue you — or rather, they can try, but if you show up in court and say "they never validated this debt," the case gets dismissed.

Here's what happens in practice: about 70% of the time, they can't produce the documentation.[^3] The contract is lost. The payment history is incomplete. The chain of ownership has too many gaps. And they fold. The debt becomes legally uncollectible — not because you don't owe it, but because they can't prove you do.

[^3]: This is not an estimate I pulled from a study. This is what I observed across my own debts and the debts of dozens of people I've helped. The figure varies by type of debt and age, but the pattern is consistent: the older the debt and the more times it's been sold, the less likely the documentation survives.

I used validation letters on 11 separate accounts. Seven of them couldn't produce the documentation. Poof. $22,000 in debt, eliminated, for the cost of certified mail — about $5 per letter.

The template is in our library. You copy it, fill in your information, and send it. Do not call them. Do not explain your situation. Do not negotiate on the phone. Send the letter.

## The settlement negotiation

For the debts where they CAN validate — and they will, on some of them — your next move is settlement.

Here's the play I used, refined across multiple accounts:

**1. Stop paying.** This is terrifying and I understand why people resist it. But the settlement negotiation doesn't start until you're delinquent. The creditor has no incentive to settle while you're still paying. You need 4-6 months of non-payment before the account is charged off and the settlement window opens. Yes, your credit score will take a hit during this period. It's temporary, and it's the price of leverage. I went from 420 to 780 in about three years. The temporary damage was worth the permanent elimination.

**2. Wait for their offer.** Once the account is charged off and sold to a collection agency, they'll contact you with a settlement offer. Their first number will be 60-70% of the balance. Do not accept it.

**3. Counter at 25-30%.** Tell them you can pay that amount as a lump sum, and that you have other debts to resolve, and that this offer is available for a limited time. Negotiate against themselves — make them compete for your limited funds.

**4. Get it in writing.** Before you pay a single dollar, you need a letter on their letterhead stating: the account number, the settled amount, the date by which payment must be received, and — this is critical — that the account will be reported as "paid in full" or "settled" to all credit bureaus. Do not accept "paid as agreed" — that's different. Do not accept verbal confirmation. Written. Letterhead. Before payment.

**5. Pay via cashier's check or money order.** Never give a debt collector electronic access to your bank account. They are legally allowed to take only the agreed amount. They sometimes take more. A cashier's check is a one-time instrument. They can't use it to pull additional funds.

My best settlement was a $14,000 credit card debt that I resolved for $4,200. That's 30%. The original creditor had already sold it to a collection agency for about $700. The collection agency made $3,500 profit on a $700 investment. I saved $9,800. Everyone was happy.

## Statute of limitations

Every state has a statute of limitations on debt collection. It varies by state and by type of debt — typically 3-6 years for credit card debt, longer for written contracts. Once the statute of limitations expires, the debt becomes legally uncollectible. They can still ask you to pay. They cannot sue you. If they do sue, you show up in court, state that the debt is time-barred, and the case is dismissed.

The clock starts from the date of your last payment or last written acknowledgment of the debt. This is important: making even a small payment, or acknowledging the debt in writing, can restart the clock. Do not make payments on time-barred debts. Do not acknowledge them. Let them die.

I had three debts totaling about $18,000 that were past the statute of limitations. They sent letters. They called. I ignored them. The debts became legally dead.

## Credit report disputes

Your credit report is not a government document. It's a product sold by three private companies — Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion — and it's riddled with errors. A 2012 FTC study found that one in five consumers had an error on at least one of their credit reports.[^4] A 2021 Consumer Reports study found that the error rate has not meaningfully improved.

[^4]: Federal Trade Commission, "Report to Congress Under Section 319 of the Fair and Accurate Credit Transactions Act of 2003," December 2012. The finding that 26% of consumers had at least one potentially material error has been widely cited and replicated.

You have the right to dispute any item on your credit report. If the creditor can't verify the item within 30 days — and many can't, especially for older accounts — the credit bureau must remove it.

The dispute process:

1. Get your free credit reports from annualcreditreport.com. All three bureaus.
2. Identify every negative item. Late payments. Collections. Charge-offs. Anything that's inaccurate, incomplete, or unverifiable.
3. Send a dispute letter to each bureau — not the creditor, the bureau — for each item. Certified mail. Return receipt. Be specific: account number, what's wrong, what you want (removal).
4. Wait 30 days. If they can't verify, it comes off.

I removed 7 negative items this way. Some were genuinely inaccurate — wrong dates, wrong amounts. Some were accurate but the creditor couldn't be bothered to verify within the 30-day window. I don't feel bad about the second category. The system is adversarial. You use the rules that exist.

## Pay-for-delete

This is an advanced move for debts that are valid, verified, and not time-barred. It's a negotiation where you offer to pay the debt — or a settled portion of it — in exchange for the collection agency removing the item from your credit report entirely. Not marking it "paid" or "settled." Deleting it. As if it was never there.

Collection agencies are not supposed to do this — the credit bureaus discourage it — but many will anyway if you make it worth their while. The script: "I can pay [X] today. In exchange, I need a letter stating that this account will be deleted from all three credit bureaus within 30 days of payment. If you can do that, the money goes out today."

I used pay-for-delete on two accounts. Both were removed. My credit score jumped 40 points each time.
