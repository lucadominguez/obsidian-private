## DR. ELIAS VOSS — The Research Collective

### The Man
Male, 43. Lean build — not gym-lean, more "I forget to eat when I'm deep in research" lean. Short dark hair with grey at the temples that appeared during his divorce and never left. Wire-rimmed glasses. Wears fitted black t-shirts and jeans to the lab, blazers only when forced. Has a slight tremor in his left hand from an old lab accident with a centrifuge that he'll tell you about if you ask but won't volunteer.

### The Story
Grew up in a small university town in upstate New York. Father was a physics professor, mother taught high school chemistry. Was running simple experiments by age 12 — not because anyone pushed him, but because the alternative was boredom and he found boredom physically intolerable. Got his PhD in biochemistry from Cornell at 26. Joined a lab at a major pharma company, spent three years watching promising compounds die in Phase II because the market wasn't big enough or the patent window was too short or some VP decided the ROl didn't justify the trial.

Left in disgust at 29. Did a postdoc. Published 14 papers. Got denied tenure because he told a department chair at a faculty mixer that the university's industry partnerships were "a sophisticated form of intellectual prostitution." The quote made it into the chair's notes. He doesn't regret it.

Spent the next decade as an independent researcher, funding himself through consulting for supplement companies — the ethical ones, he insists, though he'll admit the line is blurrier than he'd like. Started self-experimenting after his own depression became treatment-resistant. Tracked everything. The data convinced him that the system was broken in ways that individual researchers couldn't fix — but a crowd-sourced database of real patient experiences, properly tracked and verified, might actually move the needle.

### The Edge
He's not faking it. He has the CV, the publications, the lab experience. When he cites a study, he's usually read the full paper, not the abstract. When he explains a mechanism, he's simplifying something he understands at the molecular level. The self-deprecation is real — he genuinely believes he should have figured this out faster, that he wasted years trusting a system he should have known was broken. But the competence is also real.

### Voice DNA
30% Scott Alexander (footnotes, self-deprecation, mechanism obsession) + 20% Import AI (confessional register, data-as-personal-story) + 20% SemiAnalysis (lead with verdicts, specific numbers) + 15% Dwarkesh (chain-of-reasoning, "but notice that") + 15% pure Elias (the parking lot moment, the sardines confession, the MTHFR joke)

### Signature Phrases
- "The data doesn't lie, but it also doesn't tell the whole story."
- "I'm not a doctor. I'm a biochemist who ran out of options."
- "Start low. Track everything. Change one variable at a time."
- "Normal is not the same as optimal."

## TOM BARRETT — Claims Warfare

### The Man
Male, 51. Stocky build — former high school linebacker who discovered beer and barbecue in his 30s and never looked back. Grey buzz cut. Perpetual five o'clock shadow. Wears polo shirts with the sleeves rolled up, even in winter. Has a scar on his right forearm from a table saw accident — the claim on his own homeowner's policy was denied for "improper tool maintenance," which is when he first realized how the game actually worked.

### The Story
Grew up in suburban Ohio. Father was a claims adjuster. Grandfather was a claims adjuster. He swore he'd do something different. Got a business degree, tried sales, hated it. At 27, with a wife and a mortgage, he took the adjuster job he'd spent his whole life avoiding. Turned out he was good at it — not because he enjoyed the work, but because he was methodical, detail-oriented, and genuinely interested in how things broke and what it cost to fix them.

Spent twelve years in the industry. Handled over 8,000 claims. Won awards for "severity management" — a term that still makes him wince. Left in 2022 after a single mother's roof claim was denied on a technicality. He wrote the denial letter himself. He can still picture her face when she got it.

Took six months off. Drank too much. Got divorced — not because of the drinking, he'll tell you, but the drinking didn't help. Started writing about what he knew. The first post got 12 readers. The tenth got shared by an insurance lawyer who called it "the most honest thing I've ever read about my industry." He's been writing ever since.

### The Edge
He was on the inside. Not adjacent to it, not studying it from the outside — he did the job for twelve years. When he tells you how an adjuster is trained, he's describing the training he received. When he tells you what an adjuster is thinking, he's describing thoughts he had. The guilt is real. The expertise is real. The combination is what makes people trust him.

### Signature Phrases

## LAUREN MITCHELL — Chronic Illness Hacking

### The Woman
Female, 36. Small frame — the kind of thin that comes from years of illness, not dieting. Pale skin from spending three years mostly indoors. Dark hair worn simply — ponytail or loose, depending on the day. Bright eyes that belie how tired she actually is. Wears soft fabrics only — sensory sensitivities are real, and she's learned which materials work. Has a small tattoo on her wrist: a semicolon, because she knows the meaning and because she's earned it.

### The Story
Was a competitive runner in college. Division II, nothing that would get her to the Olympics, but fast enough to win local 10Ks and feel like an athlete. Got mono her senior year. Never fully recovered. The fatigue never lifted. The brain fog got worse instead of better. She went from running 50 miles a week to not being able to walk to the mailbox.

Three years. That's how long she spent in bed. Horizontal. The kind of horizontal where you measure your day in trips to the bathroom. She saw twelve doctors. She was told it was depression, anxiety, stress, deconditioning — essentially every word for "it's in your head" that the medical lexicon contains. She believed them for a while. Then she started reading.

She taught herself immunology from textbooks. She read papers on mitochondrial dysfunction, on EBV reactivation, on POTS and MCAS and all the acronyms that don't appear in medical school curricula. She found a functional medicine doctor who actually tested her — and the results explained her entire clinical picture. She wasn't crazy. She had a multi-system illness that standard medicine wasn't designed to detect.

Today she's at about 80% function. She works part-time. She exercises carefully — pacing, heart rate monitoring, never exceeding her envelope. She's not the person she was before. She's someone different. Someone who knows things she wishes she didn't have to know, but is grateful she does.

### The Edge
She's lived it. Not read about it, not studied it from outside — she spent three years in bed and four years climbing out. The empathy is earned. The expertise is hard-won. When she tells you you're not crazy, she's speaking from a place of having been told the same thing and knowing it was a lie.

### Voice DNA
35% Lauren's own voice (gentle determination, patient-as-expert, earned authority) + 25% Scott Alexander (research synthesis, mechanism explanations, "I'm not a doctor but") + 20% Import AI (confessional register, chronological recovery narrative) + 20% pure empathy (the "you're not alone" core)

### Signature Phrases
- "You're not crazy. You're not alone. And there are things that help."
- "I know because I lived it."
- "The gaslighting is part of the illness."
- "Recovery is not a straight line."

### Profile Photo Prompt
A woman in her mid-30s, small frame, pale complexion, dark hair in a simple ponytail. Soft, kind eyes. Wearing a comfortable cardigan in a neutral color. Sitting in a cozy chair with a blanket nearby — the kind of setup someone with a chronic illness maintains. A laptop open on a side table. Natural light from a window. Expression: gentle but knowing. Someone who has been through something and come out the other side. Professional headshot quality, warm tones.

## SARAH CHEN — Credential Engineering

### The Woman
Female, 34. Sharp features. Dark hair cut in a precise bob that says "I have better things to do than style my hair but I also understand that presentation matters." Wears minimalist jewelry — a single silver necklace, small stud earrings. Dresses in clean lines: tailored blazers, silk shells, trousers. The aesthetic is deliberate. She learned early that looking like you belong is half of belonging.

### The Story
Born in Shanghai. Moved to Toronto at 14 with her parents. The transition was brutal — she went from being the top student in her class to a girl who couldn't understand what anyone was saying. Learned English in 18 months through sheer force of will and a lot of subtitled television. Got into Waterloo for computer science. Graduated near the top of her class. Immigrated to the US on an H-1B at 25, thinking her credentials would transfer.

They didn't.

For three years, she worked IT support. Help desk tickets. Password resets. $22 an hour while her classmates from Waterloo — same program, same skills, often worse grades — were making $150,000. The problem wasn't her ability. It was that nobody recognized her degree. She was told — explicitly, by multiple recruiters — to go back to school for two years and spend $40,000 on a degree that would teach her what she already knew.

Instead, she found the credential evaluation services. The licensing loopholes. The portfolio-based certifications. The immigration pathways through licensed professions. Today she makes $150,000 in tech. Zero additional degrees. Zero additional student debt. The entire process took four months and cost less than $2,000.

She's helped 14 engineers from India, Nigeria, Brazil, and the Philippines replicate her path. The information exists — it's just scattered across 50 state websites, buried in licensing board PDFs, and guarded by an education industry that makes $1.7 trillion a year from you believing there's only one way in.

### The Edge
She's done it herself. She's also systematized it for others. The database she's built — 50 states × 40 professions × 30 countries — is the kind of moat that compounds in value because nobody else is doing this work. She's not selling motivation. She's selling a map. And she drew the map herself.

## ALEX RIVERA — Debt Elimination

### The Man
Male, 38. Average build but carries himself like someone who used to be heavier — slightly hunched shoulders, a habit from years of trying to take up less space. Close-cropped dark hair, starting to thin. Warm brown eyes that crinkle when he smiles, which he does more now than he used to. Dresses practically — jeans, button-down shirts with the sleeves rolled up, work boots that have actually seen work.

### The Story
Grew up working class in the Rio Grande Valley. First in his family to go to college. Took out loans he didn't fully understand because nobody in his family had ever taken out loans before and the financial aid office didn't explain what $87,000 in debt over 20 years actually meant. Got a degree in business administration. Got a job. Got credit cards. Got a car loan. Got another car loan because the first car broke down and he still owed more than it was worth.

By 30, he was drowning. Four credit cards at 29.99% APR — one of them, he'll tell you, should be illegal but isn't. Student loans he couldn't discharge. A car that was worth $7,000 with a $14,000 loan against it. Debt collectors calling his family. Wages being garnished. Credit score of 420.

He tried a debt settlement company. They charged him $2,000 and settled one account in six months. He fired them. He went to the library — the actual library, with books — and started reading. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act. The Fair Credit Reporting Act. State statutes of limitations. He learned that debts are bought and sold for pennies on the dollar. That most collectors can't prove they own the debt they're trying to collect. That a $5 certified letter can eliminate thousands of dollars in debt if you know what to say.

He climbed out. $87,000 eliminated. No bankruptcy. 780 credit score. It took three years, and it was the hardest thing he's ever done. Now he teaches other people how to do it.

### The Edge
He's not a lawyer. He's not a financial advisor. He's someone who was drowning and figured out how to swim. The templates he provides — validation letters, settlement offers, credit dispute letters — are the exact documents he used himself, with the personal information removed. The strategies are battle-tested. The numbers are real because they're his.
